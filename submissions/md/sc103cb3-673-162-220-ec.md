本人就政府就規管網約車服務的 4 項附屬法例提出意見，重點關注如何真正發
揮共享經濟優勢、減輕司機及平台負擔，以及確保規管切合實際需求。以下為
具體意見：
1. 網約車牌照數量限制過於保守，未能發揮共享經濟潛力
政府目前擬定的牌照數量過少，未能配合香港市民對靈活、便捷交通服務的實
際需求。共享經濟的核心在於充分利用閒置私人車輛及司機資源，增加道路供
應彈性。若牌照數量過嚴，將嚴重限制網約車的規模效應，無法有效紓緩公共
交通高峰期壓力及偏遠地區的交通空白。
建議：大幅增加初期牌照配額，並設立清晰、可量化的牌照擴充機制，根據實
際使用率及需求數據每半年檢討一次，讓市場力量真正發揮共享經濟的優勢。
2. 政府對司機工作時數及接單量的估算過於主觀及不切實際
政府假設每位司機每日只工作 6 小時、每小時僅 2 程，這個估算明顯過度簡化
及隨便。高峰期（如早上上班、傍晚下班、週末及節日）與平靜期的需求差異
極大，以單一平均數字作為規管基礎，既不科學，亦未能反映真實營運情況。
建議：採用更彈性及數據導向的規管模式，例如以實際 GPS 數據或平台後台
數據作為基礎，設定「每日最高工作時數」而非僵硬的每日平均接單量要求，
讓司機可根據市場需求靈活調整。
3. 司機參與成本必須盡量簡單、方便及低門檻
要吸引更多私家車主及兼職司機參與，參與程序及成本必須極度簡便。過高的

行政要求、繁複的申請步驟或高昂的合規成本，只會阻礙普通市民參與，違背
共享經濟「人人可參與」的理念。
建議：
•

簡化牌照申請及續牌程序，推行一站式網上申請平台；

•

降低或豁免不必要的行政費用；

•

提供清晰指引及過渡期支援，讓有意參與的車主容易上手。

4. 平台已具備足夠的服務質素及價格控制能力
網約車平台本身已透過評分系統、司機背景審查、行程追蹤、乘客投訴機制及
動態定價等工具，有效控制服務質素及價格。過度由政府介入細微的營運細
節，不但增加行政負擔，亦可能窒礙平台持續優化服務的動力。
建議：政府應以「平台負責、事後監管」為原則，重點監察嚴重違規個案，而
非事前全面干預平台已能有效處理的範疇。
5. 保險成本應主要由平台負擔
網約車營運的額外保險成本不應全部轉嫁給個別司機。平台作為服務中介及主
要受益者，理應承擔大部分或全部商業保險責任（包括乘客及第三方責任險），
才能真正保障司機及乘客安全，亦可避免小型司機因保費過高而卻步。
建議：明確規定平台必須為註冊司機提供或補貼相關商業保險，並公開透明地
披露保險安排，讓司機及公眾安心。

結語
網約車規管應以「促進創新、保障安全、便利市民」為目標，而非過度限制市
場發展。本人作為 Tesla Model Y 車主，對參與網約車服務（包括 Uber Black
等高端服務）有濃厚興趣，亦深信只要規管設計合理，網約車可為香港交通生
態帶來正面貢獻。
懇請委員會認真考慮上述意見，制定更務實、具彈性及以市場為本的附屬法
例，讓共享經濟在香港真正落地生根。

Subcommittee members,
I write to express three brief points regarding the proposed limit on the number of
ride-hailing vehicle permits ("vehicle permits").
(a) Goal should be maintaining the existing level of service
The existing ride-hailing service works reasonably well for many passengers.
While I acknowledge and agree that ride-hailing services need to be regulated, the
goal of setting any limit on the number of vehicle permits should be to maintain
the existing level of service – one should not fix something that works. The
number of vehicle permits should not be set at a level that would lead to something
lower than the existing level of service. Existing providers' views, so long as they
are supported by their operating data, must be given due regard because of this.
(b) A higher number is reversible; a lower number is detrimental
Setting a higher number would not be detrimental, as it can be revised downward
based on dynamic assessment and operational data, which assessment is
emphasized by the Administration. However, setting a lower number would be
detrimental, because the existing level of service would be affected.
(c) A vehicle permit is attached to a vehicle
Any limit calculation must bear in mind the limitation of a vehicle permit – that a
permit is attached to a specific vehicle. This means actual service capacity is
further constrained by vehicle availability, utilization rates, and the part-time
nature of ride-hailing vehicle drivers and their turnover. A numerical limit that
appears adequate on paper may translate into significantly lower real-world service
levels once these factors are accounted for.

Thank you.

Subject: Submission on Proposed Ride-Hailing Vehicle Permit Quota
To the Legislative Council Subcommittee,
I am writing to respectfully express my concerns regarding the proposed subsidiary
regulations that would impose a strict quota of 10,000 vehicle permits for all ridehailing services in Hong Kong, allocated through a lottery-based mechanism with
dynamic adjustments. While I recognize the importance of regulation to ensure
safety, accountability, and fair competition, I believe this specific proposal would
have unintended negative consequences for consumers, drivers, and the broader
transport ecosystem.
1. Impact on Consumer Choice and Service Availability
Hong Kong residents increasingly rely on ride-hailing services for flexibility,
convenience, and accessibility. A rigid cap of 10,000 permits risks constraining
supply well below actual demand, particularly during peak hours, adverse weather,
or in districts underserved by taxis. This would likely result in longer wait times,
higher surge pricing, and reduced reliability for passengers. Limiting consumer
choice in this way undermines the progress Hong Kong has made in modernizing
its transport options.
2. Economic and Employment Considerations
Ride-hailing platforms provide income opportunities for thousands of drivers,
many of whom value the flexibility to work part-time or supplement other
employment. A lottery-based allocation system would arbitrarily exclude capable
drivers, reducing livelihoods and discouraging participation. Moreover, restricting
supply could stifle innovation and competition, ultimately harming consumers
through higher costs and fewer service improvements.

3. Concerns with the Lottery-Based Allocation Mechanism
A lottery system does not guarantee that permits will be allocated to the most
qualified, safe, or reliable drivers. It introduces randomness into a sector where
public safety and service quality should be paramount. A merit-based
system—evaluating applicants on driving record, service standards, and
compliance—would better serve the public interest and ensure accountability.
4. Transparency and Fairness in Dynamic Adjustment
While the proposal includes a “dynamic adjustment mechanism,” the details
remain unclear. Without transparent criteria tied to measurable demand,
adjustments could be slow, insufficient, or politically influenced. This risks
creating a rigid system that fails to respond to real-time consumer needs. A
demand-driven model, where permit numbers expand or contract based on usage
data, would be more equitable and sustainable.
5. Public Interest and Transport Integration
Ride-hailing services complement Hong Kong’s public transport network by filling
gaps in coverage, especially late at night or in areas with limited taxi availability.
They provide mobility for families, elderly passengers, and those with specific
accessibility needs. Restricting supply undermines this integration and reduces
resilience in the overall transport system. A more flexible regulatory framework
would allow ride-hailing to continue supporting public mobility goals.
6. International Comparisons
Other global cities have adopted regulatory models that balance oversight with
flexibility. For example, some jurisdictions set safety and insurance requirements
without imposing arbitrary caps, allowing supply to adjust naturally to demand.
Hong Kong risks falling behind if it adopts overly restrictive measures that
discourage innovation and limit consumer benefit.

Conclusion
In summary, while regulation of ride-hailing services is necessary, a strict cap of
10,000 permits combined with a lottery allocation system is not in the best interests
of Hong Kong residents. It would reduce consumer choice, increase costs, limit
driver opportunities, and weaken the city’s transport resilience. I respectfully urge
the Legislative Council to consider alternative approaches that prioritize safety,
accountability, and demand-driven flexibility, rather than arbitrary numerical
limits.
Thank you for considering this submission. I trust that the Council will adopt a
framework that supports innovation, protects consumers, and ensures Hong Kong
remains a leader in modern urban mobility.
Sincerely,
Johan Sevenster
