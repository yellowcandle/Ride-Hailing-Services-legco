Common complaints include refused rides, cherry-pickingpassengers during peak hours, and poor driver attitudes.The entry of ride-hailingplatforms like Uber introduced long-overdue accountability to the market:Two-Way Rating Systems: Drivers and passengers rate each other. This creates a self-regulating incentive for courteous and safe behavior.Upfront Pricing and DigitalRouting: Pre-calculated fares and GPS routing eliminate overcharging andarbitrary detours.Cashless Transactions: Digital payments offer a seamless, secure,and modern alternative to traditional cash transactions.Shielding standard taxisfrom this competition removes their incentive to innovate or improve. Embracingride-hailing platforms forces the entire point-to-point transport sector to elevate itsstandards. The consumer wins.3. Boosting Transport Quantity and Meeting PeakDemandHong Kong’s public transport system is highly efficient, but it faces severecapacity strains during peak windows. During heavy rainstorms, Friday rush hours,or late-night periods after trains stop running, finding a standard taxi is notoriouslydifficult.An artificial cap on ride-hailing permits will replicate the exact supplybottlenecks that plague the traditional taxi system. Mainstreaming ride-hailingplatforms offers crucial structural advantages:Dynamic Supply Matching: Ride-hailing relies on flexible, part-time, and full-time drivers who enter the marketexactly when demand spikes.Geographic Coverage: Ride-hailing algorithmsefficiently dispatch vehicles to underserved areas outside core commercialdistricts.Relieving Mass Transit Pressure: A robust ride-hailing fleet provides areliable backup network, easing pressure on the MTR and bus networks duringdisruptions.4. Protecting the Livelihoods of Flexible WorkersThe ride-hailingeconomy supports thousands of drivers who rely on platforms for flexible income.Many drivers are self-employed individuals, parents, or part-time workersbalancing multiple responsibilities.Imposing an overly restrictive permit cap willdestroy these flexible livelihood opportunities. It risks driving the serviceunderground or forcing drivers out of work entirely. A fair and inclusive permitsystem protects these workers, brings them into a legal framework, and ensuresthey contribute positively to Hong Kong's economy.5. Recommendations for theSubcommitteeTo ensure the subsidiary legislation serves the public interest, Irequest the Subcommittee to consider the following recommendations:Maximizethe Permit Quota (L.N. 63 of 2026): Set the initial limit on Ride-hailing VehiclePermits high enough to absorb the existing pool of active, highly-rated drivers. Donot artificially choke the market.Implement an Automated, Data-Driven QuotaReview: Do not rely on static caps. The Transport and Logistics Bureau should usereal-time platform data (such as wait times and unfulfilled ride requests) to adjustpermit numbers dynamically based on public demand.Streamline the PermitProcess: Ensure that the permit application fees and bureaucratic requirementsoutlined in L.N. 61 of 2026 are reasonable. They should not act as a barrier toentry for individual, everyday drivers.6. ConclusionHong Kong aims to be aleading international financial center and a champion of smart city technology. Arestrictive approach to ride-hailing contradicts these ambitions.By keeping ride-hailing licenses widely accessible, the Government will foster a competitiveenvironment that naturally improves public transport quality and quantity. I urgethe Subcommittee to prioritize passenger choice, commuter convenience, andmarket innovation when vetting these pieces of subsidiary legislation.
