一萬網約車牌成「三輸」局面
寧思雋 思家投資
原文刊於《信報財經新聞》2026 年 5 月 29 日
網約車發牌數目最終由政府拍板：一萬試住先。美其名為「中間落墨」，但卻未
設立牌照數目調整及檢討時間機制，實際上是政府、司機及乘客的「三輸」局
面。
籠統計算運力 無視實際需求
網約車的定位，理應與傳統的士不同，在政府向立法會提交的討論文件，已有
詳細分析：約六至七成網約車司機每周工作少於 20 小時，只有約 15%接近全
職，每周工作超過 40 小時。兼職司機上線提供服務比較彈性，流動性也甚高，
同一年營運的司機中，近半數不會在翌年提供網約車服務。
明明網約車是一個充滿彈性及流動性的行業，政府卻偏偏要籠統地計算運力，
認為「三輛網約車的合併運力大致等同一輛的士運力」，然後計劃發行一萬個網
約車牌照，理由是假設每名網約車司機平均每日經營 6 小時，每小時可完成兩
個行程，每天可以合共提供 12 萬個行程，足可滿足目前的網約車需求。
然而，這種假設，猶如初中生玩數學，只看數字卻完全不顧實際情況。政府明
知道目前大部分網約車司機每周工作少於 20 小時，甚至目的只為順路載人過
海，但計算運力時卻假設司機每日經營 6 小時，意味即使以一周 5 天計算，領
牌的司機平均每周開車載客 30 小時，無視既有事實。
至於網約車每小時可以完成兩個行程，更是翻遍政府整份討論文件，都沒有提
供過的資料，到底這個數字如何計算得來？有否因應上下班高峰時段而對網約
車的需求而作出調整？一概都不清不楚。
更重要的是，為何 12 萬個行程可滿足網約車需求？政府文件中稱，預計網約車
提供的車程數目約為 11.4 萬程，數字上似乎合理。然而，相關統計調查是在
2024 年底進行，現在已是 2026 年 5 月，網約車再經過近一年半的發展，以及
近期訪港旅客增加而更為流行，同時網約車合法化後將吸引更多乘客選用等潛
在需求，政府通通無視，用過時的數據預計未來的需求。政府犯下如此基本的
估算錯誤，反映發牌數目的理據與其提供的文件幾乎沒有任何關係，難免讓外
界覺得理由只是理由，真正目的只為提高的士牌價，保障的士車主及商會。
偏幫的士商會勢「愈搞愈亂」
事實上，一旦網約車合法化，的士車行或車隊或許還有方法「托起牌價」：只要
聯合新界一些棄置車場，由車行派出的士司機，棄置車場派出仍可通過驗車的
錢七，即可爭奪大量網約車牌照，搶得牌照後即時「雪藏」，錢七即劏，牌照費
用一千幾百猶如垃圾，但可大幅壓低網約車供應，乘客被迫轉用的士，車主因
而加租，的士牌價同樣照升不誤。

政府幾乎用法例，把網約車重新定義：由過去大部分司機以兼職為主，改為
「平均駕駛 6 小時」
，兼必須完成指定行程數目，把網約車全職化，而且也保留
不少操作漏洞，甚至給予的士車行有積壓網約車牌照的想像空間，讓實際經營
網約車的數目必然比預期更低。
結果就是，政府網約車合法化，卻讓乘客被迫重新搭的士，兼職司機反而更難
經營，政府偏幫的士商會，而且幫到盡，令民怨進一步上升，可謂繼「巴士安
全帶之亂」後，又一「愈搞愈亂」的交通政策。

局長及相關官員：
就政府近日提出有關新網約車規管的方案，本人作為關注網約車發展的市民，經深思熟慮後，決定致函 貴局
反映業界與基層司機的真實困境，並對現行方案中的發牌數量、申請資格及抽籤機制提出強烈質疑，望 貴局
能認真審視。
現時方案存在以下三大核心問題，極有可能引發公共交通市場混亂，甚至打擊基層生計：

一、 10,000個牌照配額嚴重不足，未符市場現實
政府擬初階段釋出 10,000 個網約車牌照，這數字與現時龐大的市場需求及實際從業員人數相比，簡直是杯水
車薪。目前網約車已成為市民及遊客出行不可或缺的輔助交通工具，強行壓低牌照數目，只會導致「供不應
求」，最終受苦的是等車時間延長、被迫承受貴車費的廣大市民。

二、 發牌機制存在漏洞，恐出現「佔用額度而不營運」
方案「不排除任何人申請」的門檻，將誘發嚴重的投機行為。除了現有的網約車司機外，現有的士司機、甚
至並不打算全職營運的普通牌照持有者，都極大機會參與申請。部分人申請成功後，未必會真正投入網約車
服務，反而導致珍貴的牌照限額被浪費與「綁架」，無法真正解決市民「搭車難」的問題。

三、 抽籤方法極不公平，扼殺現職司機生計
政府傾向以「抽籤」方式分配牌照，此舉極不合理且缺乏科學依據。


養家糊口的生計： 現時有大量全職網約車司機（如 Uber 司機）完全依賴這份收入來供樓、養家、供
書教學。他們多年來默默為市⺠提供優質服務。



抽籤如賭博： 若將這些司機的⽣計與「純碰運氣」的抽籤掛鉤，⼀旦落空，無數家庭將⾯臨即時失
業。相反，純粹投機、志在「抽中觀望」的⼈卻可能搶佔資源。這做法對⼀直付出勞⼒的現職司機極
不公平。

我們的強烈訴求與建議
為確保政策能平穩過渡，既能規範市場，又能保障基層市民生計，本人促請政府落實以下調整：

1. 實施「現有活躍網約車司機優先發牌」制： 政府應以過去⼀段時間（如過去⼀⾄兩年）的營運數據
為依據，將首批牌照優先發放給有高活躍度、無嚴重投訴紀錄的現任網約車司機。這能確保牌照落入
真正有營運需求、有經驗的服務者手中。
2. 剔除投機者，設立嚴格營運監管： 建立「牌照回收及取消機制」。若中籤者在獲發牌後⼀定期限內
（如三個月）未能達到指定的最低營運時數，政府應即時收回牌照，並補發給候補的現職司機。
3. 因應市場彈性增加配額： 10,000 個牌照應視為「首階段過渡」，政府必須承諾在規管實施半年⾄⼀
年內，因應市場實際供求及市⺠滿意度，盡快檢討並放寬配額上限。
網約車規管的核心目的應是「提升市民出行體驗、創造公平營商環境」，而非「一刀切」扼殺基層全職司機
的生存空間。無數網約車司機正眼巴巴等待政府一個合情合理的交代，希望 貴局能本著「以人為本」的施政
理念，認真傾聽業界聲音，修訂上述不合理的發牌機制。

主席及各位委員：
本人為新入行的本港前線的士司機，對點對點道路交通服務的實際營運有深
切體會及看法。作為行業的新鮮血液，本人對日後轉型為合規網約車司機、
乃至投身自動駕駛客運產業均抱持開放及務實的態度。現得悉小組委員會正
審議政府提交的 4 項與規管網約車服務相關的附屬法例（法律公告第 61
號至第 64 號），特此提交以下結構性政策建議：

一、 引入「在線使用率」與「超額空閒補貼」機制
現時社會輿論聚焦於《道路交通（網約車車輛許可證限額）公告》中關於首
階段 10,000 個許可證剛性上限的討論，引發各界對運力足不足夠的劇烈爭
議。本人認為，引入「在線使用率監管」與「超額空閒補貼」能妥善解決此
限量爭議。在此機制下，容許參與營運的許可證總量不設限制，但平台需要
按照即時市場需求，自行調配上線車輛總量，並且承擔因估算錯誤、供過於
求所招致的超額補貼後果，真正將運力數量的爭議留給自由市場調節。

1. 落實 70% 在線載客率與分級處罰：政府應將「70% 在線載客使用率」列
為網約車平台營運條件，規定平台所有在線營運車輛，維持七成時間處於實
際載客狀態。
*核心優勢：此舉能有效促使平台利用演算法精準限制上線人數，從源頭根
治車輛空駛巡遊，切實紓緩由此引致的核心路段交通擠塞。
*處罰機制：不達標之平台應面臨按日處以高額罰款、暫停繁忙時段「動態
加價」資格、削減 30% 上線配額，情節嚴重者將直接吊銷營運執照。

2. 建立「超額空閒時間收入對沖補貼」機制：此機制以每小時為單位進行
精細化管理：
*基準設定：在一小時的在線時間內，基於 70% 載客率標準，司機標準載
客時間應為 42 分鐘，空閒接單時間上限為 18 分鐘。當平台演算法調配失
當，導致司機在每小時內的空閒時間超出 18 分鐘，超出部分即定義為「超
額空閒時間」。
*執行與補貼方式：若司機出現超額空閒，且當小時實際收入低於本港交通
行業之期望每小時收入（例如每小時港幣 200 元），平台按比例向司機補
足該超額空閒時間的差額補貼（例如超出 12 分鐘則按比例折算補償港幣
40 元）。相反，若司機因接獲高車資訂單而令當小時整體實際收入超越期
望線，則收益自動對沖，平台無需補貼。此機制能確保平台為自身調配失當
承擔經濟責任，杜絕盲目擴張。

3.落實服務優先與新手動態配額保障：為免平台在管控上線總量時出現演算
法黑箱操作，應規範平台建立公平透明的准入機制。平台可按照司機的服務
評級與投訴記錄，優先核准其上線營運申請，藉此營勵司機主動維護車況並
提供優質專業服務；與此同時，平台於各營運時段內應預留固定比例（如
15% 至 20%）的專屬配額予完全無紀錄的新人，保障新入行人士享有平等獲
取基本營運機會的權利，並得以累積服務評級，促進業界健康流動。

二、 增設「物聯網動態數位車牌系統」，落實硬體級源頭執法與全民監督
有效的政策需配套低成本、高效率的執法手段。為徹底根治非法的士營運及
平台放行超額車輛的漏洞，政府應引入現代物聯網技術，建立硬體級別的動
態監管網絡：

1. 安裝前後擋風玻璃「動態數位車牌」：規定所有獲准提供網約客運服務
的非的士車輛，於車輛前後擋風玻璃的指定位置，安裝由政府統一發行、聯
網管理的數位顯示車牌。
2. 實施政府網絡遠端激活與時限配額控制：該數位車牌的營運授權完全由
政府網絡系統遠端動態激活。此系統是強調與彰顯網約性質的核心手段。
唯有司機處於在線狀態、成功獲得上述上線配額且在合規營運時段內，其數
位車牌方會顯示「營運許可中」的特定視覺標誌。一旦司機切換至離線狀態
或營運時段屆滿，數位車牌即時遠端註銷並熄滅顯示，車輛隨即在物理上與
體制上即時失去一切點對點客運營運資格，回歸普通私家車身份。 此舉從
底層技術上硬性鎖死平台的違規調配，便於路面警員及電子執法相機進行高
效的實時視覺化執法，徹底杜絕灰色營運空間。
3. 引入二維碼即時驗證機制以落實全民監督：數位車牌上應顯示動態更新
的官方二維碼。乘客在登車前或在車廂內，可隨時利用智慧手機掃描該二維
碼，即時連線至運輸署數據庫，驗證該車輛的即時營運資格、司機身份、平
台派單紀錄以及商業營運保險狀態，落實「全民即時查核」以築牢安全防線
。

三、 修訂發牌規管：實施「人車硬性綁定」，徹底剷除「車租尋租剝削」
本港點對點交通行業長期存在牌照壟斷與層層轉租的結構性流弊。大批量租
車公司及資本機構持有營運牌照並轉租予前線司機，透過收取高昂車租獲取
非營運的尋租利潤，嚴重壓榨了前線司機的勞動成果，導致基層司機超時工
作卻回報微薄，徹底扭曲了行業的公平分配機制。

為徹底根治此行業宿疾，是次審議中有關平台司機與車輛的資格規管中，應
明確確立「人車硬性綁定」的規管原則。所有新發放的網約車營運許可，其
持有人與實際駕駛員應為同一人（即一人一車模式）。有關牌照應僅開放予

具備合格資歷的獨立自僱從業員申請，嚴禁任何平台、租車公司或財團大批
量持有牌照並轉租牟利。此機制能有效杜絕資本階層壟斷資產並藉此獲取壟
斷溢價的非生產性尋租行為，打破長久以來的資本壟斷，將點對點客運的合
理回報重新還富於真正付出勞力的前線司機，重塑健康且可持續的交通營運
生態。

四、 落實「15分鐘預約限制」與「雙向法定底線」機制
在劃分的士與網約私家車的營運範疇時，政策制定者不應只看到網約車中「
網（網絡平台技術）」的特性，更要高度強調網約車中「約（預約、約定客
運）」的本質特性。的士的核心職能與特權在於提供「即時性」出行需求
，而網約車在體制上應定位為「預約型」客運。模糊兩者邊界將導致市場惡
性交叉污染，因此在附屬法例中應設定價與預約防火牆：

1. 實施非的士車輛最少提早 15 分鐘預約之限制：規範非的士類網約車僅
可提供預約客運服務，乘客應至少提前 15 分鐘完成訂單預約，不應提供即
時叫車或路邊即時登車服務。
*回歸「約」的本質優勢（提供高度可預測之上車時間）：現行即時叫車系
統過度發展「網」的即時性，乘客因擔心缺車常盲目下單，導致司機過早到
達路邊違例停泊並空等，製造道路交通堵塞點；相反，有時乘客準備妥當卻
面臨附近無車。強調「約」的特性，推行最少提前 15 分鐘預約，能讓乘客
與平台建立明確的時間對齊窗口。平台大數據得以提前獲得精準的未來供需
預告，從容指派最合適的司機前往，確保「人到車到」、上車時間精準可期
，直接消滅路邊違停等客製造的交通瓶頸，亦能協助平台達成前文所述的
70% 載客率指標。
*保護的士即時客源護城河：此舉能將路邊隨即攔車、趕時間等所有即時性
出行需求，百分之百保留給傳統的士，鞏固的士行業的核心生存空間。

2. 建立「雙向法定底線」計價框架：社會輿論與網絡社群一直以「的士服
務欠佳」才對網約車產生強烈需求。然而，市場經濟的核心本質永遠無法脫
離「一分錢一分貨」的底蘊。社會大眾對網約車能提供低價優質服務不應有
不切實際的期望。政策制定者應維持足夠的經濟誘因，方能驅動網約車司機
主動投入成本去提供體面的車型、乾淨的車廂與高質素的專業服務。為了維
持市場自由競爭的活力，無需硬性鎖死平台的最高佣金抽成比例，以便各平
台能將佣金政策作為吸引司機或乘客的商業工具，最大化保留平台的市場變
現潛力。然而，政府在定價框架中應設立雙向底線：
*乘客支付底線：較同等路程的的士收費高出不少於 30%（即定 30% 溢價
），確立其升級、優質服務定位，杜絕低價傾銷搶占市場、進而導致服務質
素惡化的惡性循環。
*司機實收底線：應規定，司機在執行服務後，扣除平台抽成最終實收的每
公里及每分鐘報酬，不得低於同等路程的的士收費底線標準。在雙向底線保
障下，平台可於容許的車資差額內自由設定抽成率，既能給予平台充分的商
業靈活度與可持續的變現模型，亦能確保平台的任何商業博弈均不致壓榨到
前線司機的公平回報與血汗收入。

五、 全面取締其他形態白牌車，嚴禁持牌網約車「線下私自兜客」
規範網約車的同時，政府應全面清查並取締其餘任何形態之非法客運私家車
。為防範部分司機或平台以網約車為掩護、暗中提供非法客運服務，應明確
阻斷以下漏洞：

1. 嚴禁「獲取資格、脫離平台線下營運」：應明確禁止任何已獲發許可證
之網約車司機，在不開啟平台 App、不接受系統派單調配的情況下，於路邊
、機場、或各大口岸私自兜客或拉客交易。

2. 定性為非法客運並鐵腕票控：任何持牌網約車若脫離平台進行線下現金
或私下車資交易，一經路面警員、電子相機或掃碼舉報屬實，應即時定性為
非法客運。政府應扣留車輛、取消該車輛之網約許可證，並撤銷司機之網約
營運資格，徹底杜絕外來車輛利用灰色地帶進行不公平競爭與非法營運。

六、 長遠轉型與資產對接建言：設立的士業轉型基金，保障持牌車主資產
價值
隨著自動駕駛技術演進，點對點客運交通工具應平穩過渡至智慧城市新時代
：

1. 專款專用支持勞工轉型：政府應要求網約車平台將部分佣金收入，連同
收取的平台車程徵費，強制轉移並成立專屬的「的士業轉型基金」，落實專
款專用原則。該基金專門用作資助和培訓現有的士及網約車司機。在無人駕
駛客運全面普及後，積極協助他們晉升轉型為自動駕駛車隊的車廂安全員或
遠端控制中心操作人員，確保基層勞工不被科技淘汰。
2. 構建自動駕駛行業准入之「雙軌銜接方案」：日後營運商在本港提供無
人駕駛客運商業營運前，應按政府核定的比例，從市場上收購、或與現有持
牌車主合作，方可獲批營運資格。政府可為車主提供「雙軌並行」的出路
，靈活釋放既有牌照的資產潛力：
*資產轉讓變現機制：車主可自願選擇將持有的的士牌照，按市場公允價格
轉讓予自動駕駛營運商，實現資產轉型與穩定套現，保障多年持牌的合理權
益。
*聯營合作體系：車主亦可憑原有的士牌照，直接加盟合規的科技車隊進行
聯營合作。透過牌照掛靠或車隊分潤等商業模式持續獲取穩定收益，讓傳統
資產隨行業轉型持續產生經濟價值。

總結與權限建議

建議小組委員會採取更具彈性的「條文定大框架、發牌條款定具體數值」之

台的發牌執照條款。在實施初期，發牌條款中的預約時限應採取「由多到少

法能達致高使用率；另一方面，這能動態檢視網約車在不同預約長度下對傳
統的士行業客源的實質衝擊。這種循序漸進的動態調整，給予市場各方充分
的適應與緩衝窗口，在不造成社會劇烈對立的前提下，將運力與效率的博弈
完全交由科學管理來解決。

根治線下非法客運私兜漏洞、紓緩交通擠塞與智慧轉型」的監管框架，不僅

便。

Submitted by: Oren Tatcher, Urban Designer and Mobility Consultant, Hong Kong
Date: June 2026
Re: Road Traffic (Ride-Hailing Service) Regulation (L.N. 61 of 2026); Road Traffic (Limit on Number of RideHailing Vehicle Permits) Notice (L.N. 63 of 2026)

I have worked in urban design and mobility planning in Hong Kong and around the world for many years. I
welcome the legalisation of ride-hailing. I am troubled by how it is being done.
In my estimation, on the day Hong Kong's licensed ride-hailing regime comes into force, most of the
30,000 drivers currently serving the public will be gone. Fares will rise sharply. Waiting times will
lengthen. The service that hundreds of thousands of residents and visitors rely on will be worse,
immediately and significantly, than the illegal one it replaces. This is not a forecast of what might go
wrong. It is the predictable arithmetic of the rules as written.
Hongkongers today get around in several ways: the MTR and buses for the bulk of daily trips, taxis and
informal ride-hailing for door-to-door convenience. This layered system works reasonably well precisely
because no single mode is so scarce or expensive that people feel compelled to own and drive a private
car for everyday use. Hong Kong's relatively low private car dependency is not an accident. It reflects
decades of investment in public transport and a de facto ride-hailing market that has quietly filled the
niches taxis do not serve well: late nights, outer districts, airport runs, tourists concerned about language
barriers and the cash-only taxi system.
Legalisation was supposed to bring that last layer into the formal economy, improving safety,
accountability, and consumer protection, without disrupting what already works. Instead, the proposed
framework risks destroying the informal market faster than a functioning licensed one can replace it.
The core problem is the combination of a permit cap set at 10,000 and an owner-driver requirement. I
understand the concern about speculative permit acquisition and the need to manage the transition for
the taxi trade, but a blunt numerical cap is not the way to address either. The 30,000 or so drivers
currently operating informally are mostly not car owners. The owner-driver rule makes them ineligible
overnight. Among the car owners who might apply, many will be deterred by the compliance burden:
permit fees, dedicated insurance, vehicle inspection, a combined driving test. Part-time, occasional driving
becomes a bureaucratic undertaking. The honest prediction is that on the first day the licensed regime
operates, the number of compliant ride-hailing vehicles will be nowhere near 10,000. It may be a few
thousand. Perhaps less.
At that same moment, demand will be higher than it is today, not lower. The government acknowledges
in the LegCo Brief that legalisation will attract users who avoided the grey market: tourists who would not
use an unlicensed service, corporate travellers on expense accounts, older passengers reassured by a

regulated platform. Supply collapses; demand rises. The outcome is not difficult to predict: long waits,
high fares, and a service that feels worse to users than the illegal one it replaced.
What do people do when ride-hailing is slow and expensive? Some go back to taxis, which is fine, but taxis
already operate at capacity at peak times and do not serve all the niches ride-hailing fills. Some are forced
to take public transport, also fine, for the trips where it works. But a significant number of households
that already own a car and have been leaving it parked because the informal market made driving
unnecessary will simply start driving. This is the outcome I find most alarming as an urban designer. Hong
Kong has over 570,000 registered private cars, a fleet that has grown by more than 30% over the past
decade while the road network has barely expanded. Most of those cars are used infrequently. They sit in
car parks because their owners have reasonable alternatives. Remove or degrade those alternatives and
the cars move.
The owner-driver requirement deserves separate attention because it is, as far as I can determine, unique
to Hong Kong. Given the socioeconomic profile of car owners in Hong Kong, who are mostly economically
secure, it defies logic – why would middle- and upper-middle-class car owners need to offer ride hailing
services?
Indeed, everywhere else I am aware of, including Singapore, the UK, the US, Australia, and across cities in
mainland China, a ride-hailing driver must be licensed and the vehicle must be insured and roadworthy,
but the driver need not own the car. In China, Didi operates extensively through fleet operators who own
vehicles and deploy qualified drivers. In most cities, the natural unit is the household: a car that sits idle
while its owner is at work, driven on the platform by a spouse, adult child, or trusted friend. This model
converts a dormant private asset into a shared transport resource. It is efficient, it is socially organic, and
the proposed regulation prevents it entirely.
I have one further concern that goes beyond urban mobility. Hong Kong is working hard to rebuild its
appeal to international visitors after several difficult years. Tourists today arrive from cities like
Singapore, Shanghai, London, and Bangkok, where opening a ride-hailing app and getting a car in a few
minutes is simply how people get around. Many have already used informal ride-hailing in Hong Kong
and know it works. A licensed service that keeps them waiting twenty minutes and charges a premium
fare is not an improvement. It is a step backwards at exactly the wrong moment.
The fix is not complicated. Driver and vehicle standards, platform licensing, and accountability obligations
achieve everything the government legitimately wants without a numerical ceiling on supply. This is how
Singapore and London and Shenzhen regulate the sector. If the Subcommittee is not prepared to remove
the cap entirely, it should require a mandatory review within no more than six months of the regime
coming into force, with published data on waiting times, fares, and private car usage trends. Most
critically in my opinion, the owner-driver requirement should be revised to allow qualified drivers to
operate a permitted vehicle with the owner's consent, as is the norm in every comparable market.
Getting this wrong is not a minor regulatory miscalibration. The travel habits people form in the first year
of a new service tend to stick. If that first year teaches Hongkongers that the legal ride-hailing market is
unreliable and expensive, some of them will buy cars. Those cars do not disappear when the market
eventually improves.

Dear Sir/Madam,
I am writing to submit my formal views regarding the newly gazetted Road Traffic
(Limit on Number of Ride-hailing Vehicle Permits) Notice (L.N. 63 of 2026).
While I welcome the Government’s proactive step to establish a clear legal
framework for regulated point-to-point transport services, I strongly urge the
Administration to reconsider the initial cap of 10,000 permits and increase the limit
to 20,000 permits in the first phase. Setting the quota too low at 10,000 will result
in an immediate supply shock, causing significant friction for commuting
residents, tourists, and the overall development of Hong Kong's digital economy.
My recommendation is based on the following structural and economic factors:
1. The Operational Inefficiency of the "Owner-Driver" Rule ()
Under the proposed Road Traffic (Ride-hailing Service) Regulation, a ride-hailing
vehicle permit is strictly tied to the registered owner of the vehicle. This is
fundamentally different from the traditional taxi ecosystem, where a single taxi
license can be utilized across 2 to 3 shifts daily via rental arrangements to
maximize road time. Because a ride-hailing vehicle can only be operated by its
owner-driver, a 10,000 permit cap will yield significantly less actual "on-road
capacity" than 10,000 taxis. Doubling the limit to 20,000 is mathematically
required simply to achieve a comparable level of active, daily service availability
for the traveling public.
2. Severe Disparity with Existing Market Demand

The Government’s rationale assumes that 10,000 permits will generate roughly
120,000 daily trips based on a 6-hour workday per driver. However, this
dramatically underestimates current consumer reliance on these platforms. Industry
data indicates there are currently over 30,000 active ride-hailing drivers servicing
the Hong Kong public. Artificially choking the legal supply to 10,000 overnight
will destroy the livelihoods of thousands of part-time drivers, drastically increase
passenger wait times, and trigger chronic surge-pricing during peak commuting
hours and adverse weather events. This directly contradicts the Chief Executive's
stated objective to ensure the public's "riding experience remains more or less the
same."
3. Preventing Market Stifling and Supporting High Platform Licensing Fees
The Government intends to charge an annual platform service licence fee of
HK$1.2 million. For a licensed operator to build a compliant, high-quality digital
network while absorbing these regulatory costs, they require a sufficiently large
pool of active vehicles to achieve economies of scale. A restriction of 10,000
vehicles shared across potential competitors will stifle market viability, discourage
new technology entrants, and inevitably pass heavy costs down to consumers.
4. Fostering a Healthy, Integrated Ecosystem
A limit of 20,000 permits provides an optimal "middle ground" that satisfies public
demand while managing road capacity. It allows private ride-hailing to integrate
cleanly alongside the traditional 18,000-strong taxi fleet on digital platforms. A
healthier, larger quota will encourage complementary growth, motivating
traditional operators to accelerate their digital transformation via the new "Taxi
Fleet" () regime without leaving the public stranded due to a severe shortage of
point-to-point transport options.
Conclusion
A initial cap of 10,000 is an overly restrictive start that fails to reflect the reality of

modern consumer behavior and the structural limits of owner-driver mechanics. I
strongly urge the Commissioner for Transport and the Legislative Council to adopt
a more pragmatic approach by adjusting the initial limit specified under L.N. 63 of
2026 to 20,000 permits. This will ensure a smoother transition into a regulated,
dual-market transport ecosystem that truly serves the long-term interests of Hong
Kong.

賠亦可能出現爭議。

運輸署要求網約車每年通過車輛檢查，但在內地維修後的車輛能否通過香港
驗車標準，無法保證。政府必須立法禁止網約車出境維修，設立認可維修商
名單，強制於本港指定維修商進行保養。

三、Uber「共存論」自相矛盾

Uber在2024年公開表示「冀與的士共存」，卻於2021年收購HKTaxi、2026年
5月再收購「飛的」。口講共存，實行吞併，今天的我打倒昨天的我。政府
切勿被公關說話誤導，必須看清其壟斷陰謀。

四、建議

1. 限制單一外國平台市佔率不超過30%。
2. 強制平台利潤留在香港，繳付足夠稅項。
3. 立法禁止網約車出境維修，設立指定維修商制度。
4. 首階段牌照數目大幅降至2,000至3,000個，分批發放。

香港不應淪為外國平台的「提款機」，更不應犧牲道路安全和乘客保障。政
府必須果斷出手，防範壟斷，守住維修監管底線。

擴張與香港碳中和目標及鐵路骨幹規劃之間的根本矛盾；第七，分析網約車

入的士保險計算的建議，以達致風險合理定價及保費平衡；第八，總結不宜
過多發出網約車牌照的主要理據。
本人期望貴委員會能從城市長遠發展及公共政策協調的高度，審視此項關乎
香港交通未來走向的重要立法。

第一部分：的士業界十年不公平競爭的實況

一、九成半司機無牌營運：法律執行長期失衡
Uber 自 2014 年 6 月正式進入香港市場。此後長達十二年，政府從未增發出
租汽車許可證，限額一直維持於 1,500 個 。與此同時，Uber 平台自行公布，
其在香港擁有逾 30,000 名活躍網約車司機 。[5][6]
以簡單算術推算：
無牌司機數量 ≈ 30,000 − 1,500 = 28,500 人
無牌比例 ≈ 95%
換言之，市面上每 100 名透過 Uber 平台接單載客的司機，約有 95 人並未持
有出租汽車許可證。對一個每日承載約 11.4 萬程點對點交通服務的平台而

言，相關營運長期建立在大規模無牌運作之上 。相對之下，的士業界則必須
嚴格遵守《道路交通條例》（第 374 章），在牌照數量、車費結構、司機資
格及車輛保險等方面承擔全面法定責任 。同一市場中存在兩套制度：守法者
受限於配額，違規者卻得以借助平台技術擴張，這種制度落差值得嚴肅正
視。[6][5]

二、執法數字有限：違規成本不足以形成阻嚇
以下執法數據，足以反映現行監管的效果有限 ：[7]
 2015 年至 2023 年（共九年）：警方共採取超過 500 次針對非法出租或
取酬載客的執法行動；
 2019 年至 2023 年間，共 311 宗個案涉及私家車或輕型貨車，當中 275
輛被法庭定罪；
 2024 年全年：警方緝獲 47 宗涉嫌非法載客取酬個案；
 2025 年（截至 9 月）：警方緝獲個案增至 97 宗，較 2024 年全年增加
逾倍，其中 39 宗已定罪。
若以 30,000 名活躍無牌司機作比較，即使按 2025 年首九個月最高執法數字
計算，97 宗個案亦僅約佔無牌司機總數的 0.32%。這顯示現行執法機制對非

法營運者的阻嚇作用仍然有限。當合法業界多年承受客源流失與收入下滑，
而非法經營者卻可長期持續營運時，政府有必要重新檢視監管與執法架構是
否足以維持市場公平。

三、咪錶定價機制在平台經濟下的結構性劣勢
上述不公平競爭，不僅體現在監管落差，更反映於定價制度的根本不對等。
本人必須指出，的士業界受政府規管的咪錶收費標準，在平台經濟的動態定
價模式面前，正面對結構性不利。

1. 的士咪錶：剛性定價的制度限制
的士收費受《道路交通條例》及運輸署嚴格規管，車費結構以咪錶為基礎，
按行駛距離及等候時間計算，任何調整均須經由運輸署及立法會程序審批，
程序相對漫長，亦難以迅速回應市場變化 。以市區的士現行收費為例，首兩
公里 $29，其後每 200 米 $1.9（即每公里約 $9.5），等候時間每分鐘
$1.9 。此制度具有以下特點：[8][5]
 定價剛性：無論需求高低、時段繁忙與否，收費均維持固定，未能反映即
時市場供需；

 調整滯後：車費調整往往需時較長，難以即時反映燃油、保險及通脹變
化；
 彈性不足：難以就長途行程、偏遠地區或特定時段作差異化安排。
2. 網約車平台：動態定價的競爭優勢
相較之下，網約車平台採用演算法驅動的動態定價機制，可根據實時供需、
天氣、交通情況、距離及預計行程時間即時調整車費 。在高峰時段或高需求
地區，平台可透過加成定價提高車費，以吸引更多司機上線；在低需求時
段，則可透過優惠或折扣維持訂單量。此模式帶來數項競爭優勢：[6]
 供需即時匹配：價格訊號可即時引導司機前往高需求地區，降低乘客等候
時間；
 成本回收靈活：燃油價格波動、保險費用上升等成本，可透過定價機制即
時反映；
 司機激勵明確：高需求時段的加成定價可直接提高司機收入預期，形成更
強的供應誘因。

3. 定價不對等的市場後果
本人認為，兩種定價機制並存，令的士業界處於相對不利的競爭位置：

 在繁忙時段，網約車可透過加成定價提高車費及司機收入；的士則受固定
咪錶所限，無法同步分享需求上升所帶來的收益。
 在非繁忙時段，網約車可透過折扣、補貼或優惠券吸引乘客，而的士只能
以固定收費競爭，客源因而進一步被分流。
 在長途及偏遠行程方面，網約車可提供「一口價」或預估車費，提升乘客
的預算確定性；相對而言，的士按距離累進計算，往往較難在價格感知上
形成優勢。
 在司機收入層面，網約車司機可因時段及需求而獲得更高單程收入，而的
士司機則受固定定價限制，即使市場需求上升，收入上限亦難以同步提
高。

4. 不對等競爭的制度性問題
本人必須強調，的士咪錶定價並非業界自主選擇，而是政府為保障消費者權
益及防止濫收車資而設立的法定制度。的士業界承擔了接受價格規管的公共
責任，卻須在同一市場中與不受相同規管的網約車平台競爭，形成制度上的
不對等。這不僅影響業界收入，更反映法定定價制度在平台經濟下所面對的
結構性壓力。

第二部分：新界及大嶼山的士所承受的不成比例損害
本人認為，政府在考慮網約車規管安排時，必須特別正視新界的士及大嶼山
的士將承受的不成比例影響。

一、法定營運範圍的限制
根據運輸署資料，新界的士只可在指定新界區域及若干指定路線和地點行
走，而大嶼山的士更只限於大嶼山、赤鱲角及港珠澳大橋香港口岸公共運輸
交匯處營運 。換言之，新界及大嶼山的士本身已受法定營運範圍限制，其客
源空間、調配能力及營運彈性原本就較窄 。[9][10][11][12]

二、競爭條件的不對等
如網約車制度容許相關車輛在更廣泛地區行駛及接單，其營運範圍明顯較新
界及大嶼山的士更闊，競爭條件並不對等 。在此情況下，網約車對新界及大
嶼山的士所造成的影響，於制度初期未必可以準確估計，但可以合理預見的
是，該等影響將進一步壓縮新界及大嶼山的士原本已有限的生存空間 。
[10][11][12]

三、單向競爭壓力的形成
本人尤為關注，當機動性更高、行駛範圍更廣的網約車進一步分流相關地區
客源時，新界及大嶼山的士卻無法以同等方式擴展營運範圍作出回應，最終
只會形成單向競爭壓力 。這不僅對前線司機收入造成衝擊，亦可能削弱偏遠
地區及特定區域原有的士服務穩定性，對整體交通秩序未必有利 。[11][12][10]

四、本人的建議
基於以上分析，本人建議在附屬法例中明確加入以下保障措施：
 營運範圍對等原則：若網約車獲准在全港範圍內營運，當局必須同時檢討
及適度放寬新界的士及大嶼山的士的營運範圍限制，以維持基本競爭條件
對等。
 偏遠地區服務補償機制：若短期內未能調整營運範圍，政府應考慮為新界
及大嶼山的士設立專項補貼或服務津貼，以補償其因網約車競爭而承受的
不成比例損失。
 牌照數量區域配額：在設定網約車牌照總量上限時，應一併考慮各區域的
交通需求及現有的士服務覆蓋，避免網約車過度集中於新界及大嶼山等特
定區域，加劇對該等區域的士的衝擊。

第三部分：「疆屍的士牌照」問題與發牌模式的改革建議

一、「疆屍的士牌照」令原有運力消失
本人關注到，的士業界長期存在「疆屍的士牌照」的問題。所謂「疆屍牌
照」，是指部分持有的士牌照的車主，因各種原因（如牌價高企寧願持有作
升值、缺乏司機承租、車輛維修成本過高等）而未有將牌照投入實際營運，
導致相關的士長期閒置或只作有限度服務 。這些牌照名義上存在，實際上並
未為社會提供任何運輸服務，令原有的士運力白白流失。[13]
本人認為，這種狀況對社會整體而言屬資源浪費。一方面，社會對點對點交
通服務存在殷切需求；另一方面，大量合法牌照卻因制度僵化而未能有效轉
化為實際運力。若政府在規管網約車時，只顧增發新牌照而漠視現有的士牌
照的閒置問題，便等於一方面任由合法運力沉睡，另一方面另行建立全新的
發牌體系，既不符合資源效益，亦可能變相鼓勵投機性持有牌照的行為。

二、反對「先分配名額、後完成車輛登記」的發牌模式
本人注意到，有多名公眾人士在其意見書中表示，擔心在取得許可證前先購
買或登記合規車輛，將造成重大沉沒成本風險，因此建議採取「先分配名

額、後完成車輛登記、再發正式許可證」的分階段發牌模式 。本人對此不能
同意。[14]
第一，任何受規管行業的入場安排，本來就需要承擔合規成本，而不應要求
制度倒過來替經營者消除商業風險。的士車主在購入的士牌照及車輛時，同
樣需要在投入大量資金後方可正式營運，承擔市場風險及經營不確定性。若
換成網約車，便要為其度身訂造一套「先留位、後買車」的保障機制，這本
身已屬制度性優待，與公平競爭原則不符 。[14]
第二，有關意見書在「沉沒成本」一點上的論述，本身亦存在矛盾。網約車
一直以所謂「共享經濟」作為理念包裝，核心說法是透過互聯網平台善用原
本處於閒置狀態的私家車資源，讓車主在空閒時間提供載客服務，從而提高
車輛使用率及媒合供需效率 。若然如此，制度設計的出發點理應是利用現有
閒置車輛，而不是要求申請人在尚未獲證前先投入大量資金購買新車。既然
平台標榜「共享閒置資源」，又何來「沉沒成本」的憂慮？這種矛盾，反映
部分網約車支持者的真正訴求，並非共享經濟，而是以最低門檻進入受規管
的載客市場，將商業風險轉嫁至制度承擔 。[5][14]
第三，若採用「先分配名額、後買車」的模式，將衍生投機炒賣名額的風
險。一旦名額與實際車輛脫鈎，名額本身便可能成為炒賣標的，偏離發牌制

度的原意。本人憂慮，這將重演部分城市曾出現的情況，出現「有牌無車、
有車無牌」的混亂局面。

三、參考日本個人的士制度的改革建議
本人認為，與其在網約車發牌問題上疊床架屋，不如把握是次規管契機，從
制度根本上解決的士牌照的結構性問題。本人建議參考日本「個人的士」制
度的運作模式，將的士牌照的擁有權與車輛的營運權分離，達致牌主與車主
的雙贏局面 。[15]

日本個人的士制度的運作模式
日本「個人的士」制度允許符合特定條件（如駕駛經驗豐富、無重大違規紀
錄等）的個人司機，透過租用或取得牌照的方式，使用自有車輛提供的士服
務。牌照的擁有權與車輛的營運權可以分離，牌主可將牌照出租予合資格的
司機，司機則可使用符合規格的個人車輛提供載客服務 。[15]

香港的應用建議
本人建議，政府可研究修訂相關法規，在香港引入類似的分離模式：
 牌照與車輛分離：將的士牌照的擁有權與的士車輛的營運權分開。牌照持
有人可選擇自行購車營運，亦可將牌照出租予合資格司機。

 牌主可收取牌照租金：牌主可透過出租牌照獲得穩定租金收入，毋須承擔
車輛折舊、維修及日常營運管理成本與風險。這將有效激活現有「疆屍牌
照」，令沉睡的牌照重新投入市場，增加實際運力。
 司機可租牌購入合適車輛：有意入行的司機（包括現有網約車司機）可向
牌主租用牌照，然後購入符合規格的車輛（包括電動車或無障礙車輛），
正式合法提供載客服務。此舉可大幅降低入行門檻，並鼓勵司機使用更
新、更環保、更適合乘客需求的車輛。
 車輛規格由法規訂明：政府可為持牌載客車輛訂明統一的安全、環保及無
障礙標準，不論是的士還是持牌網約車，均須符合相同規格要求，體現公
平原則。

此模式的雙贏效益
本人深信，此模式可同時解決多個當前困局：
 對牌主而言：提供穩定租金回報，釋放閒置牌照價值，毋須賤賣牌照；
 對司機而言：可以較低成本合法進入載客市場，選擇適合自己的車輛，保
留營運彈性；

 對乘客而言：更多合法車輛投入市場，縮短等候時間，車隊質素可望提
升；
 對政府而言：毋須另起爐灶建立全新的網約車發牌體系，可將網約車司機
逐步納入現有規管框架，實現平穩過渡；
 對社會而言：活化閒置牌照資源，提升整體運輸效率，減少因增發新牌照
而帶來的道路壓力。

第四部分：網約車服務必須全面配合《殘疾歧視條例》
本人認為，網約車規管不僅是交通政策議題，更關乎社會平等與共融。政府
在制定網約車發牌條件及營運要求時，必須確保網約車服務全面配合香港法
例第 487 章《殘疾歧視條例》的規定，保障殘疾人士平等使用交通服務的權
利 。[2][3][4][1]

一、現行的士服務在無障礙方面的不足與努力
本人認同，本港的士服務在無障礙方面仍有改善空間。目前市面上可供輪椅
上落的無障礙的士數量有限，且預約安排分散，殘疾人士往往需要提早數小
時甚至數天預約，未必能即時應付日常出行需要 。的士業界一直支持政府推

動無障礙運輸，亦願意承擔相應責任；然而，由於無障礙車輛改裝成本高
昂、維修保養費用較高，加上相關資助計劃覆蓋有限，令車主改裝意願不
高，供應增長亦相對緩慢 。[3][16][2]

二、網約車平台的無障礙責任不應被豁免
本人必須指出，網約車平台若只以「科技公司」或「中介平台」自居，而不
承擔實質無障礙責任，並不符合公平規管原則 。目前各大網約車平台在香港
普遍未有系統性提供無障礙車輛選項，亦未建立輪椅使用者專屬的預約或配
對機制，更未有清晰服務標準去保障殘疾乘客的即時出行需要。若此種狀況
在規管框架下被合法化，實際上將令殘疾人士在點對點交通服務中持續處於
弱勢，構成結構性不平等 。[1]

三、法定責任與合理便利
根據《殘疾歧視條例》及平機會相關資料，服務提供者不得在提供服務或設
施時歧視殘疾人士；若無合理原因而拒絕提供服務，或只提供較差的服務，
即屬違法 。條例所要求的，不只是形式上的「可使用」，更包括在合理範圍
內提供實際可行的便利安排，讓殘疾人士能夠在平等基礎上享用服務 。本人
認為，這項責任同樣適用於網約車平台及其司機，不能因平台屬數碼營運模
式而獲得豁免。[4][17][2][1]

四、本人的具體建議
基於以上分析，本人建議在附屬法例中明確加入以下有關無障礙服務的規
定：
 無障礙車輛最低比例：要求網約車平台在獲得牌照後的指定期限內，確保
平台上的活躍車輛中，至少有特定比例屬可供輪椅上落的無障礙車輛，並
設有逐步提升的時間表。
 強制設立無障礙預約選項：要求所有持牌網約車平台必須在手機應用程式
內設立清晰易用的無障礙車輛預約選項，包括篩選無障礙車輛、顯示預計
等候時間、備註司機協助上落車需要，以及設立專門客戶服務熱線處理殘
疾人士查詢及投訴。
 司機培訓要求：要求網約車司機，特別是駕駛無障礙車輛的司機，必須接
受協助殘疾乘客的基礎培訓，內容包括輪椅使用者上落車程序、與不同殘
疾類別乘客的溝通技巧、《殘疾歧視條例》下的法律責任及平等對待原
則，以及處理導盲犬陪同乘客的安排。根據政府及平機會資料，拒絕導盲
犬陪同進入可供公眾使用的服務及設施，可能構成違法 。[18][19]
 拒絕服務的記錄及舉證責任：若網約車司機以車輛不適合運載輪椅或其他
殘疾相關理由拒絕提供服務，平台必須記錄具體原因並向監管機構定期匯

報。監管機構應有權抽查及調查相關個案，以確保司機並非以不合理理由
拒絕殘疾乘客。
 與的士業界的協同效應：建議將網約車無障礙車輛納入現有「可供輪椅上
落的的士」資助及支援機制，並研究建立統一的無障礙車輛預約平台，涵
蓋的士及網約車，讓殘疾人士可一站式預約合適車輛，提升整體服務效
率，並透過擴大無障礙車輛數目形成規模效應，降低每輛車的改裝及維修
成本 。[16]

五、的士基本配備應同步適用於網約車
本人亦認為，既然的士作為持牌公共交通工具，已被要求逐步提升無障礙與
乘客服務標準，則網約車在制度上亦應承擔同等基本配備責任。換言之，凡
屬的士服務應有的基本無障礙配備，例如方便輪椅上落的結構設計、清晰識
別、便利乘客上落的扶手及相關協助安排，亦應成為網約車的最低合規要
求，而不能只要求的士承擔、卻容許網約車以「平台自由」為由避開同類責
任 。[16][1]

第五部分：全球六大城市比較——高鐵路覆蓋城市的網約車定位
本人對東京、首爾、新加坡、台北、柏林及香港六個全球最出色大眾運輸城
市進行了比較研究，分析「鐵路覆蓋率高」如何影響網約車市場規模，以及
為何同屬高覆蓋城市，網約車數量仍呈現明顯差異 。[20]

核心發現
研究顯示，鐵路覆蓋率愈高，城市居民的日常通勤愈傾向由高載客量公共交
通承擔，從而壓縮網約車作為主體通勤工具的空間。在高鐵路覆蓋城市中，
網約車通常不會消失，而是由「主幹運輸工具」轉化為「補充型、彈性型及
時段型服務」。網約車市場最終規模並非單由鐵路決定，而是受法規門檻、
城市形態、道路供給、私家車持有成本及夜間與旅遊需求等因素共同塑造 。
[20]

若僅以人口或經濟規模推論網約車應有數量，往往會忽略鐵路網絡成熟度與
政策制度安排所造成的結構性差異 。[20]

六城綜合比較
 東京：超高密度、多營運者整合鐵路網 ；網約車主要壓縮於日常通勤以
外的補充需求；整體屬補充型市場。[21]

 首爾：首都圈廣域電鐵覆蓋強 ；大部分通勤由鐵路承接；夜間經濟與跨
區需求仍然存在；屬效率型補位市場。[22]
 新加坡：高覆蓋 MRT 與高公共運輸依賴 ；日常需求被公共運輸大量吸
收；高規管與高擁車成本使供應受雙重壓縮。[23]
 台北：核心都會區覆蓋高，外圍相對較弱 ；核心區叫車需求受抑制；郊
區、夜間及跨區補位需求仍然存在。[24]
 柏林：U-Bahn、S-Bahn、區鐵共同覆蓋 ；通勤需求部分被吸收；城市
較分散、法規較嚴；供需受制度限制。[25]
 香港：高人口與就業覆蓋、高公共運輸使用 ；日常依賴網約車程度較
低；但新規管框架、深夜與直達需求令市場受政策影響特別大。[26]

關鍵啟示
六城經驗共同說明一個命題：鐵路愈強，網約車愈傾向由主體交通工具退居
為補充型服務；而市場最終規模，則取決於法規制度、空間結構、道路政策
及情境型需求如何與鐵路系統互動 。真正值得關注的是，高鐵路覆蓋是否同
時配合高規管、高轉乘便利、高公共交通文化與高用車成本——若答案為
是，網約車通常只會維持在小而重要的補充市場 。[20]

對香港而言，港鐵網絡覆蓋全港約 75% 人口居住地區及約 85% 就業機會 ，
公共交通使用文化成熟，日常出行對網約車的基本依賴度本就不高。香港網
約車議題之所以突出，原因不在於城市缺乏公共交通，而在於高峰時段候車
體驗、跨區直達便利性、深夜需求及個人化服務需求仍然存在市場。這意味
香港網約車的功能定位，理應是填補公共交通的縫隙，而非取代其主體地
位。[26]

第六部分：網約車無序增加與香港碳中和及鐵路骨幹規劃的抵觸

一、與碳中和目標的矛盾
香港已把二○五○年前達致碳中和列為整體政策目標，運輸界別約佔全港總
碳排放約兩成 。《香港氣候行動藍圖 2050》亦把綠色運輸列為核心減碳策
略，目標是在二○五○年前達致車輛零排放與運輸界別零碳排放 。本人認
為，在此背景下，任何新增道路交通供應的安排，都不能只從乘客便利或市
場競爭角度評估，而必須同時檢視其對總車公里數、道路擠塞及公共交通分
擔率的長期影響 。[27][28][29]

網約車無序增加，可能從以下方面削弱減排成效：首先，網約車並非只有載
客行程，還包括接單前移動、空駛、等候及跨區調度等非載客里程；若供應
增長缺乏節制，實際道路使用量未必與有效載客服務成比例上升，反而可能
帶來更多低載客率的機動車行程 。其次，若門到門網約車供應過度擴大，部
分原屬公共交通的旅次便會轉化為較低載客效率的個別化汽車出行，公共交
通分擔率下降，道路系統承受更高壓力。碳中和講求的不僅是單車減排，還
包括整體運輸系統是否以更少能源完成更多客運工作；若大量低載客率車輛
取代高載客量鐵路與巴士，即使單車排放下降，系統效率仍可能惡化 。[30][31]

二、與鐵路骨幹規劃的衝突
香港交通政策長期強調「以公共交通為主、以鐵路作為骨幹」。最新公布的
《運輸策略藍圖》亦重申，香港運輸規劃應以公共交通為核心，並透過「八
縱八橫」基建布局、強化鐵路及主要幹道、以及推動需求導向智慧公交等措
施，持續提升整體運輸效率、出行體驗與可持續發展水平 。在路面空間有
限、交通管理必須兼顧空氣質素與城市承載力的前提下，本港運輸政策理應
繼續維持公共交通主導、鐵路骨幹的基本方向 。[32][33]
鐵路骨幹政策的本質，是透過將住宅、就業與商業活動集中於高可達性的車
站走廊，鼓勵市民依賴集體運輸而非道路型個別出行 。本人憂慮，若網約車

被容許無序擴張，政策訊號便會由「鼓勵轉乘與高載客運輸」轉向「鼓勵即
時、直達、個人化汽車服務」，逐步改變市民對出行方式的預期與習慣。一
旦需求側行為逐步偏離公共交通導向，即使鐵路系統本身仍高效，整體城市
交通亦可能朝向更高道路依賴、更高能耗的方向演化 。這實際上削弱了香港
多年來以鐵路主幹支撐高密度城市運作的制度基礎。[29][34]

第七部分：第三者風險保險——反對平台統括保險，倡議納入的士保險計算

一、平台統括保險方案的根本問題
網約車平台主張，將第三者風險保險核實程序由發牌階段移除，改由平台以
統括保險方式處理，理由是此舉「較有效率、較可負擔，亦較適合兼職司
機」。本人對此不能同意。[35]
第一，前置核實是載客服務的基本監管原則。合法持牌載客服務本來就應先
證明有足夠保險保障，之後才可獲准營運，而不是先給平台方便，再由平台
自行核實 。將保險核實由法定發牌程序轉移至平台內部處理，等於把公共監
管責任私有化，削弱政府對載客車輛安全網的把關能力。[35]

第二，「較可負擔」的說法刻意避開了保險最核心的原則：保費必須反映風
險。網約車在街上等客、巡遊、增加行駛暴露及接載頻率，客觀上本身就會
增加道路風險及第三者風險，因此保險公司按實際風險相應調高保費，屬正
常市場反應，而非不合理刁難 。平台以統括保險包裝成「較平、較方便」，
實際上只是將原本應由高風險營運模式承擔的成本，以集中購買方式平均化
甚至淡化。這不代表風險消失，只是將風險定價訊號模糊化，亦漠視原有汽
車保險公司因應網約車用途改變而調高保費的正當性 。[35]
第三，香港的特殊道路環境不支持照搬外地模式。即使其他地方曾採用平台
統括保險模式，亦不代表香港最適合照搬。香港道路密度高、車流複雜，本
地發牌及保險制度亦有其自身法律框架，因此政府更應堅持前置核實、責任
清晰及逐車把關 。以「效率」或「便利」為由放寬監管要求，最終只會把道
路安全風險轉嫁予整體社會。[35]

二、本人的具體建議：將網約車保險納入的士保險計算
基於以上分析，本人認為不應在保險環節為網約車平台另闢捷徑。相反，政
府應把握是次規管契機，將網約車的保險一併納入的士保險計算範圍，此舉
可帶來以下多項政策效益：

1. 擴大的士保險單基數，有效降低保費
的士保險市場長期面對保費偏高及投保困難的問題，其中一個結構性原因是
保險池規模有限，風險分散能力不足。若將合法化的網約車一併納入的士保
險體系，保險池的車輛基數將顯著擴大，有助保險公司更有效分散風險，從
而降低每輛車的平均保費。這對的士車主及司機而言，是實質的營運成本紓
緩。

2. 實現風險池統一，避免市場分割
若網約車與的士各自設立獨立的保險安排，市場將出現兩個平行的風險池，
可能導致風險較高的池（例如大量兼職網約車司機）保費飆升，或保險公司
選擇性承保。將兩者納入統一保險計算，可避免市場分割，使風險在更大基
數上得到更合理的定價與分攤。

3. 符合公平競爭原則
的士與網約車在本質上提供相同的點對點載客服務，理應在同一保險框架下
接受相同的風險評估與保費標準。統一保險安排可消除因保險成本差異而產
生的不公平競爭，體現「同服務、同風險、同保費」的規管原則。

4. 達致的士業界與網約車司機的雙贏
對的士業界而言，保費下降是直接受惠；對網約車司機而言，納入正規保險
體系可使其獲得合法的營運身份及充足的第三者風險保障，避免因保險不足
而面臨法律及財務風險。本人深信，這是一項對雙方均有利的制度安排。

第八部分：為何不宜過多發出網約車牌照
綜合以上分析，本人認為不宜過多發出網約車牌照的理據，遠不止於維護既
有行業利益，而是涉及多重公共政策考量 ：[36]

1. 市場可持續性與司機生計
過多發牌容易導致市場內卷化。當活躍車輛增幅遠高於實際新增需求時，單
車可分配訂單將被攤薄，司機需投入更長工時以維持收入，平台則可能以價
格戰進一步刺激短期供給。本人擔心，這種競爭模式可能降低司機職業穩定
性，使服務質素因收入壓力而波動 。[30]

2. 道路公共成本
香港核心區路面空間有限，若大量網約車同時進入熱門商業區及住宅區，便
會增加上落客、輪候及兜圈機會，拖慢其他公共交通與商業車輛運作。這些
成本並非由平台或乘客個別承擔，而是由整個城市共同吸收 。[33]

3. 監管能力
網約車規管涉及平台、司機與車輛三方管理，若牌照數量大幅增加，政府在
資格審查、車輛檢驗、保險查核、違規監測方面的行政負荷亦會同步增加。
本人擔心，當監管資源無法與市場規模同步擴張時，制度便容易出現名義上
有牌、實際上難以精準執法的情況 。[36]

4. 政策一致性與循證治理
網約車制度仍屬相對新近的法定框架，若在初期已發出過大量牌照，日後即
使發現數量超出承載能力，也難以在不引發重大市場反彈的情況下回收或收
緊。較合理的做法應是以總量管控為原則，配合定期檢討機制，按真實出行
需求及公共交通整體表現調整牌照數量 。[36]

5. 對新界及大嶼山的士的區域性影響
如本人前述，新界及大嶼山的士受限於法定營運範圍，若網約車牌照過多且
可全港自由行駛，將對該等區域的士造成不成比例的衝擊。牌照數量的設定
必須考慮各區域的交通服務承載力及現有的士服務穩定性的地域差異 。
[12][9][10][11]

6. 無障礙服務責任的承載力
本人亦必須指出，若網約車牌照數量在短期內大幅增加，而無障礙車輛比例
及相關培訓未能同步跟上，將導致殘疾人士在龐大的網約車市場中被進一步
邊緣化。牌照數量的設定必須與無障礙服務的推進步伐相協調，確保殘疾人
士的交通權利不會因市場快速擴張而被稀釋 。[2][1]

總結與建議
過去十年，是香港點對點交通服務法律真空、秩序混亂的十年。一個高達
95% 無牌率的客運平台在港大規模擴張，而執法數字僅觸及其冰山一角，反
映現行監管安排仍有明顯不足。的士業界在這場不對等競爭中已承受十年壓
力，其中新界及大嶼山的士因法定營運範圍所限，所受影響更為明顯；的士
咪錶定價機制在平台動態定價面前，也正面對結構性的競爭劣勢。
從全球頂尖大眾運輸城市的經驗可見，高鐵路覆蓋城市中的網約車，合理定
位應是「補充型、彈性型及時段型服務」，而非取代公共交通主體地位的道
路型替代系統 。網約車無序擴張，將與香港的碳中和承諾 、鐵路骨幹規劃 及
路面空間管理 產生根本矛盾。[28][32][33][20]
在牌照制度方面，與其為網約車另起爐灶，不如把握契機解決「疆屍的士牌
照」的結構性問題。本人建議參考日本個人的士制度，將牌價與車殼分開，
既可激活閒置運力，亦可為現有網約車司機提供合法入行途徑，實現牌主與
車主雙贏 。[15]
在社會責任層面，網約車平台長期在無障礙服務方面缺位，規管框架必須確
保網約車服務全面配合《殘疾歧視條例》的規定，保障殘疾人士享有平等交

通服務權利，促進社會共融 。同時，的士既有的基本無障礙配備與服務標
準，也應同步成為網約車的最低合規要求 。[3][4][1][2][16]
在保險安排上，平台提出的統括保險方案試圖繞過前置核實及風險定價原
則，將監管責任私有化、風險成本模糊化，不符合香港道路安全及消費者保
障的基本要求。相反，將網約車保險納入的士保險計算，既可擴大保險池基
數、有效降低保費，又可實現風險公平分攤及雙贏局面 。[35]
本人懇請各位委員在審議附屬法例時，堅守以下原則：
 發牌條件必須嚴格對等：網約車平台、車輛及司機的發牌要求，必須在保
險、司機體檢、駕駛執照門檻、稅務責任及罰則等方面，與的士業界達至
同一標準 。[5]
 設立明確的供應上限，並兼顧區域差異：牌照數量應圍繞補充性需求設
計，以總量管控為原則，避免網約車由補充服務轉化為對公共交通主體客
流的競逐者。同時，必須充分考慮新界及大嶼山的士的營運範圍限制，設
定相應的區域配額或保障措施 。[10][11][12][36]
 保障新界及大嶼山的士的生存空間：若網約車獲准在全港範圍內營運，當
局必須同時檢討及適度放寬新界的士及大嶼山的士的營運範圍限制，或設
立專項補償機制，以維持基本競爭條件對等 。[9][11][12][10]

 改革牌照制度，激活閒置運力：參考日本個人的士制度，研究將的士牌照
擁有權與車輛營運權分離，讓牌主可出租牌照、司機可租牌購車，實現雙
贏，同時將現有網約車司機逐步納入正規框架 。[15]
 全面落實《殘疾歧視條例》的平等服務要求：網約車平台必須設立無障礙
車輛最低比例、強制無障礙預約選項、司機協助殘疾乘客培訓，並嚴禁以
殘疾為由拒絕提供服務。網約車的無障礙車輛應納入政府資助計劃，與的
士業界共同分擔無障礙運輸責任，體現社會共融 。[4][1][2][3]
 堅持保險前置核實，拒絕平台統括保險方案：第三者風險保險核實應維持
在發牌階段進行，不應轉移由平台自行處理。保費必須如實反映網約車營
運模式的實際風險，不應以統括保險方式淡化風險定價訊號 。[35]
 將網約車保險納入的士保險計算：以擴大保險池基數，有效降低的士保
費，實現的士業界與網約車司機的雙贏，並體現「同服務、同風險、同保
費」的公平競爭原則 。[35]
 徹底杜絕非法營運：附屬法例必須賦予執法部門充足權力及資源，持續打
擊無牌營運。對於過去長期非法營運的平台，其「過渡期」安排不應變相
認可過去長達十年的違規行為 。[7]

 確認平台的法定責任：新法例必須明確界定平台的法律身份，確保司機獲
合理薪酬與勞工保障，消費者獲應有保障 。[36]
今日審議附屬法例，是政府與立法會撥亂反正的關鍵時刻。本人懇請各位委
員堅持「公平競爭、依法規管」的底線，明確界定網約車作為鐵路骨幹下補
充型服務的功能定位，正視新界及大嶼山的士的特殊處境，激活閒置牌照資
源，保障殘疾人士平等使用交通服務的權利，維護保險制度的風險定價原
則，切勿讓守法者長期承受不公平負擔。
謹此陳詞，懇請參酌。

SUBMISSION TO THE LEGISLATIVE COUNCIL PANEL ON TRANSPORT
RE: REGULATORY FRAMEWORK AND QUOTA SYSTEM FOR RIDEHAILING SERVICES
Submitted by: Mr. Dunn

1. Strategic Optimization of the 10,000 Vehicle Quota and Fleet Composition
While the proposed initial cap of 10,000 ride-hailing vehicle permits represents a
prudent and balanced baseline to protect local road capacity, the Government must
enforce stringent, premium-centric vehicle entry requirements. To maximize public
utility and avoid market saturation, the quota allocation should strictly prioritize
premium, high-capacity vehicle classes.
The administration must establish an unambiguous, statutory definition for "Premium
7-Seater Vehicles" based on vehicle dimensions, luxury categorization, and market
valuation:


The Inadequacy of Compact Models: Certain compact 5+2 Multi-Purpose
Vehicles (MPVs) and small hybrid station wagons (e.g., Honda Freed, Toyota
Prius V) lack legitimate third-row legroom and executive-grade luggage
capacity. Permitting these entry-level models undermines the objective of
providing a distinctly superior alternative transport tier.



Proposed Premium Criteria: Eligible 7-seaters under the quota should meet
a minimum vehicle length requirement (e.g., 4.9 meters or above) to guarantee
uncompromised third-row passenger comfort and cargo space. Furthermore, a
minimum first-hand taxable value or retail price threshold should be instituted
to ensure the fleet remains exclusively premium.



Gold Standard Benchmarks: The allocation framework should explicitly
target luxury MPVs (such as the Toyota Alphard, Toyota Vellfire, or Lexus LM)
and high-end executive sedans that align with recognized premium global
standards (such as Uber Black requirements).

2. Differentiation, Market Segmentation, and the Japan Regulatory Precedent
To maintain a stable transport ecosystem, the regulatory framework must prevent
structural overlaps between mainstream ride-hailing tiers (such as Uber X and
Comfort) and traditional taxi operations. Allowing standard ride-hailing tiers to
expand unchecked would create intense, vicious competition for the same street-level
passenger demographic, destabilizing the taxi industry and exacerbating road
congestion.


The Japan Model as Evidence: A highly successful global precedent can be
found in Japan, where authorities historically mitigated extreme taxi-industry
friction by restricting international ride-hailing platforms predominantly to
premium tiers, such as Uber Black. By strictly enforcing this boundary, Japan
carved out a distinct premium executive niche that fulfilled specialized
corporate and luxury consumer demand without cannibalizing the core
traditional taxi market.



Mitigating Manpower Shortages: Establishing a strict luxury-only barrier
means that freelance drivers using standard private cars will be ineligible for
the 10,000 ride-hailing quotas. Instead of risking illegal operations, these
highly experienced road users can be directly routed into the traditional taxi
sector. This strategy would immediately inject skilled, legally compliant
manpower into a taxi trade currently suffering from an acute driver shortage,
transforming a regulatory constraint into an immediate public benefit.

3. Loophole Mitigation: Unlicensed Travel Agency Limousine Services
A critical regulatory gap exists within the travel agency sector regarding unauthorized
chauffeur services. Due to the limited supply of legally issued Private Service
(Limousine) Hire Car Permits, a standard practice has emerged where travel
agencies hire unpermitted private vehicles to meet high tourist and corporate demand.
The enactment of the new ride-hailing regime amplifies this enforcement risk:


Displacement to Shadow Markets: Operators and drivers who fail to secure
a permit under the competitive 10,000 ride-hailing quota will face a strong
economic incentive to shift their operations to travel agencies. They will

continue providing illegal point-to-point carriage under the guise of "travel
packages" or "corporate rentals."


Increased Enforcement Deficits: Because travel agencies bundle these
unauthorized transport services into broader tour itineraries or accommodation
packages, identifying, auditing, and prosecuting illegal carriage for hire
becomes exceptionally difficult for law enforcement.



Public Safety and Liabilities: Vehicles operating without authentic Hire Car
Permits operate outside commercial third-party passenger insurance mandates,
exposing international tourists and local citizens to severe financial and legal
liabilities in the event of an accident.

Consequently, the new ride-hailing licensing regime must be accompanied by
rigorous, cross-departmental enforcement targeting travel agencies utilizing
unpermitted private vehicles to prevent the expansion of an unregulated shadow
market.
4. Mandatory Data Transparency and Protection of Driver Autonomy
To guarantee operational safety and protect the core professional freedoms of
participants, any approved ride-hailing software application operating in Hong Kong
must be statutorily required to provide full trip transparency to the driver before an
offer is accepted. The application interface must explicitly reveal the exact fare
payout, estimated trip duration, and specific destination.


International Precedents: This regulatory model is well-established globally.
In major transport jurisdictions such as Uber United Kingdom and Uber
Australia, the platform incorporates an Upfront Pricing and Fare system that
displays clear pickup and dropoff cross-streets, alongside the exact earnings
breakdown. This guarantees that independent operators can make fully
informed commercial decisions before accepting a dispatch.



Mitigating Operational and Safety Risks: Withholding destination details
forces drivers into "blind acceptances," causing severe safety hazards. Drivers
who are unexpectedly dispatched to unfamiliar, complex, or remote terrain
may suffer from route disorientation, leading to erratic driving behaviors,

abrupt stops, and unprofessional navigation. Full upfront information enables
drivers to reject trips outside their geographical expertise, thereby elevating
road safety.


Alignment with Constitutional Rights: Under the principles enshrined in the
Basic Law (specifically concerning the right to freedom of choice of
occupation and labor protections), intentionally concealing essential
transactional details from an independent service provider is fundamentally
coercive. Withholding destination data effectively compromises a driver’s
freedom of choice, forcing them to accept unfavorable or structurally unviable
trips under the threat of platform account penalties. True regulatory
modernization must mandate upfront transparency as a baseline operating
condition for all licensed platforms.

5. Enforcing Automated Single-Offer Dispatch Systems Over Dangerous "Order
Grabbing" Interfaces
For the safety of all road users, the Government must strictly regulate how trip offers
are presented to drivers within ride-hailing applications. The legislative framework
must mandate an automated, single-offer dispatch model (similar to the native Uber
driver interface) and explicitly ban open-market "order grabbing" (competitive
bidding or speed-clicking) mechanisms commonly used by local logistics and hailing
apps.


The Extreme Danger of Order Grabbing: Order-grabbing interfaces
broadcast open trip offers to a massive pool of drivers simultaneously. To
secure a booking, active drivers are forced to constantly stare at their mobile
mounts, rapidly read fine print, and aggressively tap their screens while
navigating live traffic. This creates a severe, continuous source of distracted
driving.



The Safety Superiority of Direct Dispatch: In contrast, a standardized
dispatch model routes a single trip directly to the most optimal driver based on
proximity. The driver is given an isolated interface with large, clear prompt
areas to accept or decline the route calmly, minimizing cognitive distraction.



Statutory Safety Requirements: To obtain an operating license in Hong

Kong, platforms must prove their driver UI adheres to strict distractive-driving
mitigation standards. Interactive bidding wars or competing for orders on
screen while a vehicle is in motion must be treated as a violation of platform
licensing conditions, aligning with the intent of the Road Traffic Ordinance
(Cap. 374) regarding safe vehicle operation.
6. Algorithmic Accountability and Mandatory Auditing of Dynamic "Surge"
Pricing
Beyond spatial routing, transparency must be legally enforced regarding platform
financial calculations. The current "surge pricing" or dynamic pricing mechanism
used by ride-hailing platforms remains completely opaque ("black-box" technology)
to both riders and drivers. Neither party can verify how multipliers are calculated,
resulting in asymmetrical information control that borders on algorithmic price
exploitation.


Global Scrutiny on Surveillance Pricing: This lack of transparency has
drawn significant regulatory backlash worldwide. Regulatory bodies globally
have launched formal inquiries into platform pricing algorithms. These
investigations focus on how data—such as high localized demand or device
state—might be leveraged to inflate prices beyond fair market equilibrium,
causing distributed harm to consumers and arbitrary wage variances for
drivers.



Preventing Market and Fleet Manipulation: Opaque surge mechanics allow
a platform to unilaterally adjust its platform "take-rate" (the percentage cut the
platform retains) on a per-ride basis. This system frequently charges riders an
inflated surge premium while failing to pass a fair, proportional share of that
surge down to the driver executing the trip. Furthermore, unmonitored surge
algorithms can encourage artificial supply manipulation, where drivers
collectively disconnect from the network to trigger false shortages and force
the system into surge rates.



Proposed Legislative Safeguards: To operate legally within Hong Kong's
formalized public transit network, platforms must be legally mandated to
make their pricing algorithms accountable:

o

Real-Time Breakdown Disclosures: The application must show a
clear, real-time invoice breakdown to both the rider and driver,
explicitly isolating the base fare, distance/time fee, exact surge
multiplier applied, and the platform's exact commission rate.

o

Independent Government Audits: Platforms must submit their
dynamic pricing source code and historic fare data to the Transport
Department for periodic audits. This ensures pricing fluctuates strictly
based on aggregate macro-environmental supply and demand rather
than predatory, consumer-specific data exploitation.

7. Formal Conclusion and Recommendations
In summary, I respectfully urge the Legislative Council and the Transport and
Logistics Bureau to adopt the following measures:
1. Enact precise dimensional and financial thresholds for the 10,000-vehicle
quota to ensure it is populated exclusively by genuine luxury and largecapacity vehicles.
2. Restrict platform operations to high-end premium services (mirroring Japan’s
Uber Black strategy) to safeguard the baseline taxi market and encourage
experienced drivers to enter the formal taxi industry.
3. Strengthen legal enforcement and platform-auditing mechanisms against
travel agencies operating unlicensed, unpermitted private transport services.
4. Mandate complete upfront informational transparency (destination, fare, and
duration) on all approved ride-hailing driver interfaces, in line with established
UK and Australian frameworks, to preserve safety and operational autonomy.
5. Ban unsafe "order grabbing" bidding mechanics inside driver apps, legally
requiring platforms to utilize automated, single-offer dispatch models to
eliminate distracted-driving hazards.
6. Enforce statutory transparency and public auditing over dynamic surge
pricing algorithms to protect local consumers from predatory price-gouging
and shield drivers from opaque commission shifts.

Thank you for considering these views to foster a safe, complementary, and orderly
public transport network in Hong Kong.

As an ordinary worker, my reason for taking ride-hailing cars is very simple: for the places I need to
go for work, buses or rail either require a detour or multiple transfers1; and taxis cannot be hailed.
With ride-hailing, I finally do not have to deal with all that. When the car arrives, I get straight in. If
there are not enough ride-hailing cars, the outcome for me is simple: I cannot get a car and I cannot
get to work.
In response to the Subcommittee's public invitation for written views on these four pieces of
subsidiary legislation (Legal Notices Nos. 61 to 64), I set out my concerns as a member of the public
who relies on ride-hailing every day to get to work. The authorities say licences may be issued as soon
as the end of this November. In other words, once these regulations are passed, they will immediately
affect the daily lives of me and hundreds of thousands of people who use ride-hailing services. I hope
the Legislative Council will think carefully about the following points before passing the legislation.

1Let

me also register a complaint here: if the 606 bus route did not currently

1. There will not be enough cars; passengers simply will not be able to get one The 10,000 quota
may sound like a lot, but a calculation using the Government's own figures shows that it is not
enough. In paragraph 12 of the LegCo Brief, the Government itself says current ride-hailing trips are
about 114,000 per day. On what basis is 10,000 vehicles enough? The key is paragraph 14: it assumes
each vehicle works a full six hours per day, and only on that basis does it produce the capacity
calculation that "three ride-hailing cars are equivalent to one taxi". But paragraph 13 of the same
paper already proves that assumption wrong: 60% to 70% of drivers work less than 20 hours per
week, which averages out to only two to three hours per day; the Government itself also states in
black and white in paragraph 14 that ride-hailing drivers' "average daily working hours are about a
few hours". If a car actually works only two to three hours, its capacity is only one-third to one-half of
the Government's assumed six hours. In other words, 10,000 cars cannot even meet half of today's
demand (see Figure 1).
The Government also reassures itself in paragraph 14 by saying drivers "may increase their operating
hours having regard to different considerations such as operating costs and order demand". This
assumption is completely detached from reality. The Government's own paper, paragraph 13, says
part-time drivers choose to work only "outside the hours of their full-time jobs". That means they
already have full-time jobs and simply do not have more time. If you increase their costs, they will
just quit. Why would they work several more hours instead?
Moreover, the 10,000 cap is so small that it cannot even fit the existing drivers. Paragraph 13 of the
Government's LegCo Brief states that, for just one platform, more than 30,000 drivers accepted at
least one order through that platform within a year; and Hong Kong's ride-hailing market is not
limited to one platform. In other words, the actual total number of drivers currently doing ride-hailing
work is far above 30,000. If you impose a hard cap of 10,000 vehicle permits, you cannot even
accommodate the drivers of one platform. That is plainly pushing most existing drivers off the road.

those times, a large pool of part-time drivers who usually do not take orders but come out to help
during these periods is essential. Uber's July 2025 submission to the Legislative Council Panel on
Transport also noted that demand can vary by as much as 66% across different times of a day. To
handle fluctuations of that size, the only reasonable approach is to have a large pool of part-time
drivers able to go online and take orders when needed. Among ride-hailing drivers who are actually
taking orders and serving the public, even paragraph 13 of the Government's LegCo Brief admits that
60% to 70% are part-time. Yet the Road Traffic (Ride-hailing Service) Regulation under Legal Notice
No. 61 uses four measures, each hitting part-time drivers the hardest: (i) owner-driver binding, under
which the car may only be driven by the registered owner (LegCo Brief, paragraph 10), so driving a
family member's or friend's car or using a rental car for work is not allowed; (ii) minimum-trip
renewal, under which the vehicle must complete a specified number of trips within a specified period
before the permit can be renewed (LegCo Brief, paragraph 21), clearly designed to eliminate lowfrequency vehicles, meaning part-time drivers; (iii) one-size-fits-all fixed costs and procedures commercial insurance, permit fees and annual inspections - which are expensive and troublesome for
people who drive only a few hours a week; and (iv) a 10,000 cap, balloting, and a preference for longterm car owners, meaning new entrants and occasional drivers cannot get in. The result is this: at peak
times, when more people are competing for cars, there will be fewer cars; precisely when cars are
most needed, passengers will be unable to get one.

Figure 2: Working-hour distribution of ride-hailing drivers (data from paragraph 13 of the Government's LegCo Brief): parttime drivers are the mainstream, and the regulations hit them the hardest

Please amend:

•

Remove the "minimum trips before renewal" rule, or set the threshold extremely low so that it
only clears long-term inactive dead permits, instead of driving out part-time drivers;

•

Relax owner-driver binding: it is acceptable for the permit to be tied to the vehicle, but a group
of registered authorised drivers (for example, family members living together, or a small
number of named permit holders) should be allowed to drive the same car, instead of limiting it
to the registered owner only.

3. The fees and procedures are absurd for a part-time industry with high turnover The
Government says the fees are "close to cost". But the real issue is not the cost amount itself. The
questions are whether it is necessary to stack this whole set of requirements onto every driver, and
whether every ride-hailing driver must personally pay these costs. The problem is not only money; it
is also the entire cumbersome process of applying, buying commercial insurance, and taking the car
for annual inspection every year. Look at how absurd the numbers are: every driver must personally
pay the vehicle permit fee and buy commercial insurance that is several times more expensive than
private-car insurance, layer after layer. But paragraph 13 of the Government's own LegCo Brief states
in black and white: "The turnover of existing ride-hailing drivers is very high. Among drivers
operating in the same year, nearly half do not continue to provide ride-hailing services in the
following year." In other words, almost half of drivers leave within a year. You are asking them, just
to try it for a few months, to go through such a cumbersome process, pay so much money, and buy
commercial insurance? No one will accept that. Also, many platforms already provide insurance for
their ride-hailing vehicles; requiring drivers to buy another set themselves is duplicated and
unnecessary.
Precisely because driver turnover is so high, both the vehicle permit and the driver permit should be
transferable. If one person stops doing this work, the permit and qualification can be transferred
together to the next person who wants to do it, instead of restarting the whole process every time.
Some may worry that transfers would lead to permit speculation. But as long as the first point above is
implemented - quotas automatically increase with demand and are no longer artificially capped permits will not be scarce, there will be no speculative price, and transfers cannot be profitably
speculated. That is the only way to match the reality of high driver turnover.
Please amend:
•

Do not impose the entire stack of commercial insurance and vehicle-permit requirements on
every driver in a one-size-fits-all way. Especially for part-time drivers who drive very little or
leave within a year, the relevant procedures should be greatly simplified or even waived. Do
not regulate ride-hailing drivers as if all of them are full-time drivers;

•

Insurance should be purchased by platforms for the drivers taking orders under them. Drivers
should not be required to buy a separate set of commercial insurance that duplicates coverage;

•

Expressly allow vehicle permits and driver permits to be transferred, so that when a driver
stops working, the baton can be passed to the next driver in line with the reality of high
turnover.

4. The current licensing system benefits large platforms and shuts local new companies out
When the Government announced the regulatory framework in 2025, it already stated that it would set
an "entry threshold" for platforms: only those meeting requirements in three areas - past operating
experience, capital investment, and proof of financial resources - can apply for a ride-hailing service
licence. The "past operating experience" requirement is a dead end for new local companies: without a
licence they cannot operate; without operation they cannot obtain experience; without experience they
cannot obtain a licence. In other words, a brand-new local start-up, however capable and however
good its technology is, is blocked at the door from the beginning. The only beneficiaries are the large
platforms that have operated overseas and already have a track record. On top of that, an annual
licence fee of HK$1.2 million raises the threshold even further. The result is that the market is
dominated by a few large platforms, local innovation cannot develop, and passengers and drivers have
fewer and fewer choices. The bigger problem is that this "entry threshold" is now only verbally stated
by the Government and written in its paper. The subsidiary legislation itself does not set out specific
requirements and standards in black and white, leaving local companies that want to enter the market
at a loss, not even knowing whether they qualify or what they must do to qualify.
Please amend:
•

Write the detailed criteria and objective standards for platform "entry thresholds" (including
so-called operating experience, capital and financial-resource requirements) into the subsidiary
legislation, so that local new companies wishing to enter the market have a legal basis and
know the rules of the game, instead of being left at a loss;

•

Do not use requirements such as "past operating experience" that local new companies cannot
possibly satisfy at the beginning to block entry. New companies should be allowed to qualify
through capital, technology and a viable operating plan, and should be allowed to start small
(small platforms may first choose to operate in a specific area [for example, only within the
Science Park] or serve a specific target group [for example, boundary-control-point passengers,
wheelchair passengers, passengers travelling with pets, and so on], without having to provide
service across all of Hong Kong from day one), then expand after meeting standards. A fair
opportunity should be left for local innovation and technology;

•

Substantially reduce the HK$1.2 million annual fee for a ride-hailing service licence. Do not
use a money threshold alone to screen out small platforms.

5. Supply is already insufficient today, yet we are being told to wait slowly The target of 120,000
trips only barely meets today's demand, with no reserve at all. In fact, even counting only today's
demand, and applying the working hours that the Government itself admits, more than half of ride
requests would already fail (see Figure 3). Demand is still rising: according to an industrycommissioned study, about 19% of ride requests already fail because the wait is too long, and pointto-point demand is projected to rise to more than 1.4 million trips per day by 2034. The legislation
says there can later be "dynamic assessment and timely adjustment", but the Secretary did not even
state a review timetable (public media response by the Secretary in May 2026). A single phrase,
"dynamic adjustment", gives passengers no real protection. For people who need ride-hailing every
day to get to work, waiting is not an option. A cap that is already insufficient today, and comes with
no commitment on when it will be relaxed, is equivalent to pre-installing a bottleneck in Hong Kong's
transport system and telling the public not to go out.

Figure 3: Using demand from paragraph 12 of the LegCo Brief and working hours from paragraph 13, assuming 2.5 hours
per day, more than half of requests already cannot get a car today

Please amend: Write the licence-increase mechanism into the subsidiary legislation, with clear
indicators (failed request rate and waiting time). Once a specified level is reached, quotas should
automatically increase. The law should also provide for a fixed review cycle (for example, quarterly)
and public disclosure of review results, instead of leaving the decision solely to the subjective
judgement of individual officers or committees.

6. Self-contradiction: owner-driver binding forces people to buy cars and worsens congestion
The Government says the cap is to preserve road capacity and avoid congestion. But "owner-driver
binding" effectively forces anyone who wants to be a driver to own a car under their own name; if
they have no car, they have to buy one. The Government itself admits this in paragraph 19 of the
LegCo Brief: development of ride-hailing services "may attract persons who currently do not have
private cars to purchase vehicles ... leading to a sudden increase in the number of private cars in Hong
Kong and increasing the overall load on the road system." In other words, the Government knows its
own design will push people to buy cars and worsen congestion, yet its "countermeasure" is to give
preference in balloting to people who already own cars. That does not solve the problem; it is selfdeception (see Figure 4). In the end, even when I am stuck in a car on the road, unable to move, I will
be suffering because of this self-contradictory rule.

Figure 4: The self-contradiction of owner-driver binding (based on paragraphs 10 and 19 of the LegCo Brief and the Legal
Notice No. 61 Regulation)

Please amend: Deal with this together with the relaxation of owner-driver binding under point 2:
allow a group of authorised drivers to drive the same car, and pair this with an automatically adjusted
quota, so that a person who originally does not own a car is not forced to buy an extra private car
simply to do ride-hailing.

7. Ride-hailing should save cars and road space, but the new rules instead force more people to
buy cars The original design of ride-hailing is to allow car owners who are already driving to and
from work to take one or two passengers going the same way. The same car and the same trip, which
originally carried only one person, can now carry one or two more. For the road network, that saves
cars and road space. But the whole set of current regulations - owner-driver binding, minimum trips,
and high costs and procedures - is precisely driving away these "on-the-way passenger" car owners.
Once they leave, what can passengers do? There are only two options: either call a taxi or ride-hailing
car driven by a full-time driver, putting another commercial vehicle on the road; or, if they cannot
bear it, save desperately and buy a private car to commute. Whichever option they choose, the number
of cars on the road will only increase, not decrease (see Figure 5). The Government keeps saying it
wants to manage Hong Kong's traffic well, but what these regulations are doing is step by step
pushing more cars onto the road. That benefits no one.

Figure 5: Driving away car owners who carry passengers going the same way turns one car into two or three; the road will
only become more congested

Please amend as follows:
•

Do not use owner-driver binding, minimum-trip requirements and heavy costs to drive away
car owners who carry passengers going the same way. Keep them in the system, so one car can
carry several more people going the same direction;

•

Also, if the Government genuinely wants to reduce congestion, the legislation should expressly
allow taxis and ride-hailing cars to lawfully operate carpooling services (similar to services
such as GrabShare overseas), so that people going in the same direction can share one vehicle.
Naturally, there will be fewer cars and less congestion.

8. This legislation is also the right time to amend two outdated items (i) Section 33G(c) of Legal
Notice No. 62 (Road Traffic (Driving Licences) (Amendment) Regulation 2026) still requires people
applying for taxi and ride-hailing permits to be "able to recognise well-known places, and identify the
most suitable route between two specified places". That requirement belongs to the era decades ago
when navigation did not exist. Today, everyone has maps and navigation apps on their phone, which
can calculate the fastest route in real time and avoid congestion, and ride-hailing platforms' driver
apps also have built-in navigation functions. Forcing drivers to memorise where everything in Hong
Kong is and which road is fastest is unnecessary and deters many people who genuinely want to enter
the trade. (ii) The whole set of regulations is written around the assumption that "someone drives":
there is a driving test, and because of "owner-driver binding" the registered owner must drive
personally. But driverless ride-hailing is already in actual operation in many places around the world,
and sooner or later it will come to Hong Kong. If Hong Kong continues to regulate ride-hailing only
through "driver licences", driverless vehicles will not fit into this legal framework at all, leaving them
without a proper legal basis or forcing another legislative exercise from scratch, wasting time and
falling behind others.
Please amend:
•

Delete the route-recognition / route-finding requirement in section 33G(c). Neither taxi nor
ride-hailing drivers should still be tested on this;

•

Reserve room in the legislation now to allow future regulation by "operating platform /
vehicle", not only by "driver", so that once driverless ride-hailing matures, there is already a
legal basis and no last-minute catch-up is needed.

9. Existing drivers who are already working and have safe records should not be forced to
retake a test There are already tens of thousands of ride-hailing drivers safely carrying people
around every day. The new rules introduce a written test. Even if the Government provides a
transition period and lets existing drivers take the test gradually, requiring all of them to retake this
written test is itself unnecessary. If a driver has worked for a year, safely completed hundreds of
passenger trips and has a good platform record, whether the driver knows the roads and knows how to
do this work has already been proven by actual performance. Why force them to sit down and take a
paper test again? The more practical risk is that many ride-hailing drivers are part-time and turnover is
high. If you force them to study and sit an exam just to continue working, many will simply quit. The
result is that an experienced group of drivers who were already doing the work properly will be driven
away for nothing, supply will become even tighter, and passengers like me who need rides will lose
out.
Please amend:
•

Any existing ride-hailing driver who, before the new rules take effect, has already worked for
more than one year, safely completed more than 250 trips, and has a good platform record (no
serious complaints, no accidents caused, and no serious breaches) should be deemed to have
passed the written test, without being required to retake it, so as to minimise the impact of the
transition period.

I want to be clear: I am not against regulation, and I am not against safety. I fully support every
measure in the regulations that protects passenger safety, such as driver background checks,
thresholds based on serious traffic convictions, sufficient vehicle insurance, and strict enforcement
against illegal hire-car services, because these measures protect my own safety as a passenger. None
of them should be reduced. What I oppose are only the provisions that purely obstruct supply, drive
away drivers, make it impossible to meet public demand, and run against the objective of reducing
congestion.
In conclusion, I am just an ordinary member of the public who wants to get to work reliably and earn
a living. These regulations are packaged as "prudence", but in reality they will mean that people like
me who rely on ride-hailing to get to work cannot get a car in the morning, cannot get a car at night no
matter how many times we request one, and are eventually forced to buy a private car we cannot
afford. Also, the draft claims to reduce congestion while driving away car owners who carry
passengers going the same way and pushing more cars onto the road. It is also written only with the
old mindset of "someone drives", with no room prepared for future driverless vehicles. The
authorities' own paper admits that many drivers can only work two to three hours a day and leave
within a year, yet it assumes that after legislation they will work a full six hours and even work
several more hours. Even today's demand cannot be met, yet licences may be issued by the end of
November. I beg you: please do not rush through something that will, by your own hand, destroy our
daily lives. I sincerely urge the Subcommittee to ask the authorities to substantially amend the
provisions mentioned above before further scrutiny and licensing proceed.
