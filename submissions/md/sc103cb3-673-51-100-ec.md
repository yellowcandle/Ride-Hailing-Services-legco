I write as a member of the riding public to urge the Subcommittee to reconsider
four elements of the gazetted subsidiary legislation: the hard cap of 10,000 ridehailing vehicle permits, the lottery-based allocation mechanism, the dynamic
adjustment mechanism, and the economic impact this would have. While I
welcome the government's long-overdue effort to legalise ride-hailing, the current
design risks locking in artificial scarcity at the very moment the industry gains
legitimacy.
1. The 10,000-Permit Cap Is Inadequate and Unsupported by Published Evidence
The Transport and Logistics Bureau has described the cap as "cautious and
appropriate," yet has not published the demand modelling or operational data
behind this figure. Without that evidence, the public cannot assess whether 10,000
reflects genuine road-capacity constraints or is primarily a concession to the
incumbent taxi trade.
The Bureau's own Legislative Council paper acknowledges that the quota is
"significantly lower" than what is needed to meet rider demand, and that over
20,000 prospective drivers would be denied an opportunity to earn. A cap set
deliberately below known demand does not protect the public — it protects
incumbents. The Subcommittee should require the Bureau to publish its full
demand assessment before the Notice takes effect.
2. A Lottery Mechanism Allocates Permits Irrationally
Lotteries distribute scarce resources randomly, with no regard for service quality,
vehicle safety, driver experience, or platform reliability. A driver with an excellent
record has the same chance as a new entrant with no track record.
Better models exist. The government could prioritise applicants meeting higher

service-quality thresholds — vehicle age, driver ratings, accessibility features — or
allocate permits to platforms based on demonstrated user demand. A merit- or
demand-weighted system would serve passengers better and incentivise quality
from day one. I ask the Subcommittee to direct the Bureau to redesign the
allocation mechanism so permits flow to the most capable operators, not the
luckiest ones.
3. The Dynamic Adjustment Mechanism Lacks Enforceable Timelines and
Transparency
The government states the cap "may be adjusted over time," but the gazetted
legislation specifies no review frequency, no adjustment triggers, no publication
requirements, and no implementation deadlines. Without these guardrails,
"dynamic adjustment" is a promise with no enforcement mechanism. Hong Kong's
taxi medallion system — where supply was frozen for decades despite growing
demand — is a cautionary tale this framework risks repeating.

those less well-served by taxis — late-night travellers, people with mobility needs,

Reasonable licensing, safety standards, and insurance requirements protect
consumers. Arbitrary supply caps do not. The regulatory objective should be a
safe, competitive, and consumer-responsive market — not a managed contraction
of one.

not screen out unqualified applicants, it screens out qualified ones arbitrarily. The

3. Replace the lottery allocation with a merit- or demand-weighted mechanism that
rewards service quality and accessibility.
4. Strengthen the dynamic adjustment mechanism by writing mandatory review
timelines, publication requirements, and enforceable adjustment triggers into the
legislation.
5. Recognise driver livelihoods as a material policy consideration and require the
Bureau to publish an economic impact assessment of the permit cap before the
