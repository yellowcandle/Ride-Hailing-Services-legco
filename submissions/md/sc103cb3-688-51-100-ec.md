一、 10,000個牌照嚴重低於市場真實需求
根據業界估算，現時香港活躍的網約車司機及用戶數量龐大，遠超一萬之數
。每日繁忙時間、惡劣天氣（如颱風、暴雨）或公共交通工具收車後，市民
對網約車的需求呈現爆發式增長。若將牌照硬性限制在 10,000 個：
「動態加價」常態化：求過於供必然導致繁忙時間車費飆升，基層及中產
市民出行成本大增。
等候時間大幅拉長：市民在急需用車時（如趕往醫院、機場或商務會議
）將面臨「有錢無車搭」的困境，嚴重影響日常生活效率。
二、 硬設上限助長「牌照炒賣」歪風，重蹈覆轍
香港點對點交通面臨的歷史共業，正是源於的士牌照多年來「只減不增」且
無限期發牌，導致牌照變成投機炒賣的金融工具，最終令優質服務變質。如
果政府在網約車制度上重施故技，硬設 10,000 個上限，必然引發新一輪的
牌照壟斷與炒賣。屆時，高昂的「租牌成本」會全數轉嫁予乘客及司機，令
規管網約車、提升服務質素的初衷完全變調。
三、 打擊斜槓族（Slashing）及靈活就業人口
網約車的一大優勢在於其靈活性，不少司機屬於兼職或家庭照顧者，利用空
餘時間賺取收入。若實施嚴格的配額制，有限的牌照必然傾向全職或大財團
壟斷。這不僅扼殺了數以萬計基層市民賺取外快的彈性就業機會，亦不符合
香港政府近年鼓勵多元經濟和創新勞動力市場的政策方向。
四、 違背智慧城市與多元交通的發展藍圖
作為國際金融中心，香港一直積極推動智慧城市藍圖。網約車利用大數據配

對、路況動態導航及透明度高的評價機制，是現代科技改善出行的最佳體現
。政府規管的核心應該是**「車輛安全、司機背景、保險保障與服務質素」
**，而非倒退地用行政手段限制技術的應用與市場規模。健康的多元交通生
態，應該讓的士與網約車在公平透明的平台上良性競爭，由市場決定供求
，而非由政府預設框架。
建議解決方案：
1. 實施「動態配額制度」：政府不應定死一個硬性數字，應根據季度或年
度的市民出行數據、旅客增長及交通負荷，動態調整牌照數目。
2. 以優質管理取代數目限制：著重審查平台的營運資格、車輛的安全結構
、以及第三方責任保險的落實，只要符合安全及服務標準的車輛，皆應容許
投入市場。
期望政府能廣納民意，以「市民福祉」與「長遠交通發展」為依歸，切勿因
循守舊，因照顧個別既得利益集團而犧牲廣大市民的出行權益。

1. 重蹈供需失衡的覆轍：
過去傳統計程車（的士）因牌照數量被長期凍結，導致市民在雨天或高峰時

1. 司機資格審查：
強制平台對司機進行背景審查，確保其無暴力、性犯罪或危險駕駛等刑事紀
錄，並設定合理的駕齡要求。
2. 車輛與保險合規：
營運車輛必須購買合規的「第三方乘客責任保險」，確保發生意外時乘客能

3. 平台安全科技：督促平台利用科技手段保障安全，例如強制推行行程實
時GPS監控、一鍵報警功能及雙向匿名評價機制。

安全的紅線上嚴格執法，其餘的車輛數量、收費模式等，皆應全權交由市場
自行調節，才能真正落實「以民為本」的交通政策。

Submission on the Proposed Regulation of Ride-Hailing Services in
Hong Kong
Dear Sir / Madam,
I write to express my opposition to the proposed initial cap of 10,000 ride-hailing vehicle permits
under the proposed regulatory regime for ride-hailing services in Hong Kong.
I support the introduction of a clear and lawful regulatory framework for ride-hailing services.
Passenger safety, proper insurance coverage, vehicle inspection, driver eligibility, platform
accountability, fare transparency and effective enforcement are all legitimate and necessary
regulatory objectives. However, the regulatory framework should be designed to improve
passenger welfare, service quality and lawful market competition. It should not result in an
unduly restrictive market structure that limits consumer choice or weakens the effectiveness of
the proposed reform.
The proposed initial cap of 10,000 ride-hailing vehicle permits appears to be too restrictive.
While road capacity and coordination with the wider public transport system are relevant
considerations, those objectives should be balanced against actual passenger demand, service
availability, waiting times, fare levels, service coverage and the need for a competitive point-topoint transport market. A fixed quota that is set materially below market demand may create
artificial scarcity, reduce service availability, increase fares and limit the benefits that the
regulatory regime is intended to deliver.
In my respectful view, the proposed approach remains too conservative in light of modern
passenger expectations. App-based booking, transparent fare estimates, digital payment, route
records, driver identification, rating systems and platform-based complaint handling have
become normal features of point-to-point transport services in many modern cities. Hong Kong’s
regulatory framework should therefore be technology-neutral and passenger-oriented. Ridehailing should be treated as a legitimate and properly regulated part of the personalised pointto-point transport system, rather than merely as a limited supplement to existing taxi services.
Official data indicate persistent service-quality concerns in the taxi sector. According to the
Transport Complaints Unit’s 2024 annual data, there were 13,096 complaints and suggestions
relating to taxi services in 2024, accounting for around 29% of all cases concerning public
transport services. The number of complaints and suggestions per million passenger journeys
was also the highest among all types of public transport services. The relevant categories
included matters such as refusing hire, failing to take the most direct route, improper driving
behaviour, discourteous conduct and overcharging. These figures do not justify criticism of all
taxi drivers, many of whom provide honest and professional service. However, they do
demonstrate the need for stronger service-quality accountability and greater lawful competition
in the personalised point-to-point transport market.
Road safety should also be addressed in an evidence-based and non-discriminatory manner.
Publicly available materials have noted concerns regarding the accident involvement rate of
taxis, long operating hours and the age profile of taxi drivers. This should not be interpreted as a
criticism of individual older drivers or as a suggestion that age alone determines driving safety.
Rather, it supports the need for robust and modern safety standards applicable to all
personalised point-to-point transport providers. Such standards should include medical fitness
requirements, recurrent assessments where appropriate, driving-record checks, vehicle
inspection, driver training, fatigue-risk management and technology-enabled monitoring.

and increase supply during peak demand periods. It may also encourage higher service
standards through lawful competition. Competition should be viewed as a mechanism for public
benefit, provided that all platforms, vehicles and drivers are subject to clear and enforceable
safety, insurance and compliance requirements.
The current roadside and transport-hub arrangements also require review. Many major
locations, including airports, railway stations, ferry terminals, boundary control points, hospitals,

designated, properly managed and legally recognised pick-up and drop-off zones for ride-hailing
services where passenger demand is substantial and where traffic conditions permit.

Submission on the Subsidiary Legislation Relating to the Regulation of
Ride-hailing Services
I support the Government's initiative to establish a legal and clear regulatory framework for
ride-hailing services. However, I have strong reservations about the proposal to cap the
number of ride-hailing vehicle licences at 10,000, as I believe such an arrangement fails to
adequately address the actual transportation needs of Hong Kong citizens.
In recent years, an increasing number of residents have chosen to use ride-hailing services.
This is not merely due to pricing considerations, but because ride-hailing platforms provide
a more convenient, transparent and reliable travel experience. Passengers can access fare
estimates, driver information, expected arrival times and trip records through mobile
applications. In addition, rating systems help enhance service quality and accountability.
These are important features of modern point-to-point transportation services in major
cities around the world.
The proposed cap of 10,000 ride-hailing vehicle licences raises serious questions as to
whether it will be sufficient to meet market demand. According to recent public surveys, a
significant proportion of both passengers and drivers believe that a quota of 10,000 licences
is inadequate. During peak periods, adverse weather conditions, public holidays and major
events, many residents already experience difficulties securing transportation and face
excessive waiting times. An insufficient supply of ride-hailing vehicles will only result in
longer waiting times, reduced availability and potentially higher fares, undermining the
intended benefits of the reform.
In my view, the Government should not determine in advance, through administrative
measures, the exact number of ride-hailing vehicles required by the market. Instead, it
should establish a transparent, data-driven mechanism that allows for periodic review and
adjustment. Relevant indicators may include average waiting times, booking success rates,
passenger satisfaction levels, and supply-demand conditions across different districts and
time periods. Licence numbers should be adjusted according to actual market conditions
rather than being constrained by a fixed long-term cap.
Furthermore, there is a growing public perception that the proposed quota arrangement
places excessive emphasis on protecting existing vested interests, while insufficiently
considering the needs of the travelling public. Public policy should be guided by the

For many years, members of the public have expressed concerns regarding certain aspects

of taxi services, including refusal of hire, passenger selection, circuitous routing and
inconsistent service standards. The introduction of more legal and properly regulated ridehailing services can encourage healthy competition and drive improvements in the overall
quality of point-to-point transport services, ultimately benefiting the wider community.
I also believe that ride-hailing services and taxis are not necessarily engaged in a zero-sum
competition. With appropriate regulation and fair competition, both can coexist and
develop alongside one another, contributing to a more efficient and responsive
transportation system. As an international city, Hong Kong should embrace innovation and
align itself with the transportation trends of other major global cities, rather than
maintaining the status quo through excessive restrictions on market supply.
I therefore urge the Legislative Council and the Government to:
1. Reconsider the proposed cap of 10,000 ride-hailing vehicle licences;
2. Establish a dynamic adjustment mechanism based on actual market demand and
operational data;
3. Regularly publish service performance indicators, including waiting times and booking
success rates;
4. Avoid treating the protection of vested interests as a primary consideration in restricting
competition; and
5. Prioritise public convenience, service quality and overall social welfare as the key
objectives of transportation policy.
Hong Kong citizens need a higher-quality, more convenient and more diversified point-topoint transportation system, rather than another regulatory framework that artificially
restricts supply. I hope the Government will adopt an open, pragmatic and citizen-centred
approach in reviewing this proposal and determine future licence numbers on the basis of
objective data and actual market demand.
Public policy should not be designed primarily to preserve licence values. Its purpose
should be to enhance public welfare. What deserves protection is the travelling public's
right to efficient and reliable transportation, not any particular vested interest.
Thank you for considering my views.
