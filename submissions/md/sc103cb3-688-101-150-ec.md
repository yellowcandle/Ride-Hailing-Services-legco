Concise Submission on Ride-hailing Vehicle Licensing
I support the Government’s efforts to establish a legal framework for ride-hailing services,
but I oppose the proposed cap of 10,000 ride-hailing vehicle licences. The growing
popularity of ride-hailing services reflects genuine public demand for more convenient,
transparent and reliable transportation options.
Passengers value the ability to access fare estimates, driver information and real-time
arrival updates through mobile applications. Rating systems also promote accountability
and service quality. These features have become standard expectations in modern urban
transportation systems.
The proposed quota of 10,000 licences appears arbitrary and may be insufficient to meet
actual demand, particularly during peak hours, adverse weather conditions, public holidays
and major events. If supply is artificially restricted, passengers will continue to experience
long waiting times and difficulties securing transportation.
Rather than imposing a fixed cap, the Government should adopt a data-driven approach.
Licence numbers should be reviewed regularly based on objective indicators such as
waiting times, booking success rates, passenger satisfaction and market demand. Public
policy should prioritise the interests of citizens and overall social welfare, rather than
protecting existing vested interests.
Ride-hailing services and taxis can coexist under fair and effective regulation. Healthy
competition can improve service standards and provide greater choice for consumers. I
therefore urge the Government to reconsider the proposed 10,000-licence cap and develop
a flexible licensing framework that responds to the real needs of Hong Kong residents.

一、核心訴求
本團體/本人強烈反對政府建議將首階段網約車車輛許可證上限設為
10,000個。在現行法例已包含多項極其嚴苛的「資格性限制」下，市場已具

牌照總量限額」。
二、多重合規限制已具控量效果，另設限額屬層疊架屋
政府提交的附屬法例中，已包含三重硬性門檻，這些限制本身已發揮了強大
的篩選作用，根本不會出現網約車「無限膨脹」：
「 人 車 綁 定 」 限 制 ： 規 定 司 機 必 須 為 車 輛 登 記 車 主
。 此 條 款 已 徹 底 封 殺 「 租 車 入 行 」 空 間 ， 排 除 了 絕

「 最 低 營 運 要 求 」 限 制 ： 要 求 網 約 車 每 年 必 須 完 成
指 定 行 程 數 （ 平 均 每 日 需 駕 駛 數 程 ） ， 否 則 不 予 續
牌 ， 完 全 排 除 了 閒 置 車 輛 。

$10,000 至 $30,000 港 元 的 商 用 第 三 者 責 任 保 險 ， 連 同 許
可 證 費 用 及 車 齡 限 制 ， 構 成 極 高 准 入 門 檻 。

機被淘汰，徹底違背提升服務質素的政策初衷。

康競爭。

3. Prioritize the public's freedom of choice over the protection of a stagnant
monopoly.

Dear Sir/Madam,
I am writing to you as a regular user of ride-hailing services in Hong Kong to voice
my serious concerns regarding the government’s recent proposal to cap initial ridehailing vehicle licenses at 10,000. While I fully support the government’s initiative
to bring formal regulation, safety standards, and insurance accountability to the
sector, setting the initial ceiling this low will heavily penalize everyday passengers
and disrupt Hong Kong’s broader transport ecosystem.
As a commuter who relies on these services when traditional public transport or
taxis are unavailable, inefficient, or impractical, I urge the Bureau to reconsider the
initial quota based on the following real-world impacts:
1. Severe Impact on Passenger Wait Times and Fares
Data from existing platforms indicates that there are currently over 30,000 active
ride-hailing drivers serving Hong Kong residents and tourists. Slashing this
capacity to 10,000 means cutting the available fleet by nearly two-thirds. For
passengers like myself, this will inevitably lead to:
Skyrocketing Fares: Independent projections show that reduced supply could drive
up fares by as much as 70% during peak periods due to severe supply-demand
imbalances.
Longer Wait Times: Booking failure rates could rise dramatically—potentially
failing 4 out of 10 times during rush hours—while standard wait times are
expected to double.
2. Disruption to Hong Kong’s Image as a Global Tourist Hub

Hong Kong prides itself on being a seamless, world-class smart city. International
travelers and business visitors overwhelmingly expect reliable, app-based door-todoor transit. If tourists experience frequent booking rejections and excessive
delays, it directly damages the city's reputation for convenience and modern
infrastructure.
3. Preserving Flexible Livelihoods for Existing Drivers
A strict, low cap ignores the reality of thousands of drivers who currently rely on
these platforms for flexible, honest income. By restricting the market so abruptly,
the policy threatens to displace over 20,000 drivers overnight. I believe the
government should reward experience; any licensing framework should prioritize
active, highly-rated, and experienced drivers who have safe driving records, rather
than distributing permits through a rigid lottery system.
My Suggestions to the Bureau:
Adopt a Dynamic, Data-Driven Scaling System: Instead of enforcing a rigid,
arbitrary 10,000 cap from day one, start with a higher baseline that reflects current
market utilization (e.g., 20,000 to 25,000 permits) and adjust the quota quarterly
based on real-time data submitted by licensed platforms regarding ride demand,
fulfillment rates, and traffic impacts.
Reject Artificial Pricing Floors: I welcome the Bureau's stance against imposing a
minimum fare matching traditional taxi meters. Keeping pricing market-driven
ensures that consumers retain affordable choices.
Encourage Co-existence, Not Elimination: Ride-hailing should complement, not
replace, traditional taxis. By allowing the sector to scale naturally with demand,
the government can foster healthy competition that encourages traditional taxis to
improve their own service quality.
A thriving global city requires a modern, flexible transportation network. I urge the
Bureau to take public sentiment and real-world demand seriously, adjusting the

initial framework so that Hong Kong commuters are not left stranded by a shortage
of legal transport options.
Thank you for your time and for considering the voices of Hong Kong's
commuting public.
