本人作為香港市民，關注本港交通服務質素及市民出行需要，就現時立法會
正審議的網約車規管附屬法例，提出以下意見。
強行設定網約車牌照上限，違背市場原則，亦未能發揮網約車的最大優勢
——善用閒置資源。懇請委員們在審議時，充分考慮市民訴求，放寬或取消
數量限制，讓市場需求和公平競爭自然決定供給。
一、香港網約車規管嚴重落後，急需追上國際及內地步伐
網約車已在全球多個先進城市及中國內地廣泛推行多年，並已證明能有效提
升交通效率、降低市民出行成本及改善服務質素。例如新加坡、倫敦、紐約
及內地多個城市均已建立成熟的規管框架，允許平台在安全及合規前提下運
作。香港作為國際金融中心，卻至今仍未有全面合法化網約車，市民長期面
對的士供應不足、拒載、服務態度欠佳及高峰期難叫車等問題，嚴重影響日
常生活及城市競爭力。政府不應繼續拖延，必須加快步伐，制定務實而具前
瞻性的規管制度。
二、反對以保護的士行業為由，犧牲廣大市民福祉
的士行業長期享有牌照壟斷優勢，但服務質素問題（如拒載、繞路、態度欠
佳、車廂衛生）廣受詬病，已有大量投訴數據支持。網約車的出現並非要取
代的士，而是提供多元、互補的選擇。國際經驗顯示，透過差異化服務（如
的士提供街頭即叫即走、網約車提供預約及舒適體驗），網約車能有效提升
整體點對點交通服務水平，迫使傳統的士改善服務，形成良性競爭。政府若
過度偏袒的士，限制網約車發展，只會令市民繼續承受高價、低質、選擇少
的出行困境。這不符合「以民為本」的施政原則。
三、網約車數量不應設上限，應由市場需求決定
本人強烈建議取消網約車車輛數量上限，讓市場供求及競爭自然調節。這是

網約車模式的核心優勢之一：充分利用閒置私人汽車資源。許多私家車主在
非上班時間或車輛閒置時，可選擇兼職成為網約車司機，既增加收入，亦提
升道路資源使用效率，減少不必要的汽車購買及停泊需求。這符合環保及可
持續發展理念。
人為設定車輛配額，只會製造人為短缺，重蹈的士牌照制度的覆轍，導致黑
市、價格扭曲及服務不足。市場競爭才是確保價格合理、服務提升的最有效
機制。
香港市民早已等待太久。立法會作為民意代表，理應以廣大市民的出行便利
及生活質素為優先，而非個別行業的既得利益。希望各位議員以大局為重
，支持開放及創新的網約車政策，讓香港交通服務真正邁向現代化。
感謝各位委員審閱意見。

近年本港的士拒載、揀客等情況屢見不鮮，不僅影響市民日常出行，更損害
香港的城市形象。從馬克思主義政治經濟學剖析，此乃「生產資料」（的士
牌照）被少數資本家高度壟斷的必然結果。車行憑藉獨市優勢主導市場，長
期剝削前線司機並影響廣大市民權益。政府理應順應社會發展，將網約車發
牌數目大幅增至四萬個或以上，甚至不設上限，以實質且廣泛的競爭來改變
當前僵化的壟斷格局。
馬克思主義指出，資本具有不斷擴張並走向壟斷的傾向。本港的士牌照數量
長期凍結，早已從公共服務許可異化為資產階級的投資與炒賣工具。政府雖
嘗試推出「的士車隊」，但這類不觸及壟斷根本的改良措施難以見效。從理
論角度看，車隊制度只是資本內部的重新包裝，並未改變舊有的生產關係。
現實中車隊數目有限，市民叫車仍需面臨久候；且高昂車租的剝削本質未改
，車隊司機為求盡快回本，寧願如普通的士般在街上接客，亦缺乏誘因接納
叫車單。只要車行的獨市優勢未除，內部微調皆難以治本。唯有將網約車牌
照增至四萬以上或不設上限，方能形成足夠龐大的市場力量，促使既得利益
者面對真正的競爭與轉型。
現時的士服務質素欠佳，很大程度源於極度不平等的生產關係。前線的士司
機作為無產階級，每天須向車主繳納高昂的固定車租，承受著巨大的生活壓
力。為「追回成本」，部分司機無奈採取專揀「靚旗」或長途客等手段以保
障微薄收入。大規模開放網約車，意味著將生產資料與勞動自主權重新下放
給廣大勞動者，打破傳統車行的單一僱傭壟斷。當司機擁有充足的平台選項
與更具彈性的工作模式，車行便難以單方面不斷加租剝削。只有當勞動者的
基本待遇得以改善、勞動尊嚴得到保障，他們方有真正的餘裕與動力去提升
服務質素。
馬克思主義強調，當社會生產力發展到一定階段，必將要求改變落後的生產
關係。網約車技術正是先進生產力的體現，而現時僵化的的士發牌制度卻成

為了阻礙社會進步的枷鎖。交通運輸的根本目的是滿足群眾出行需求，而非
保障少數壟斷者的超額利潤。大規模發放網約車牌照，能有效善用科技妥善
分配資源，填補市場空缺，將過度傾斜的公共資源交還大眾。解決的士亂象
單靠加重罰則或車隊制度只是治標。促請政府盡快落實四萬個或不設上限的
網約車發牌機制，從根本理順生產關係，讓公共交通服務重回正軌。

1. Executive SummaryThis submission is written in response to the invitation from
the Subcommittee on Four Pieces of Subsidiary Legislation Relating to Regulation
of Ride-Hailing Services. The objective is to provide a constructive, supportive
view on the legalization and structured integration of ride-hailing platforms, such
as Uber, within the personalized point-to-point (P2P) transport landscape of Hong
Kong.Over the past decade, platform-based P2P transport has grown exponentially,
with over 120 million trips completed in Hong Kong since 2014, reflecting
profound changes in consumer behavior and market expectations (Secretariat,
2025c). Drawing upon empirical research from the Legislative Council Secretariat
and global transport models, this submission argues that formalizing ride-hailing
services is essential to resolve long-standing service-quality deficiencies in the
traditional taxi industry, align the Hong Kong Special Administrative Region
(HKSAR) with unavoidable global smart-city trends, and support the broader
economic revival of the city. 2. Contextualizing the Regulatory ShiftThe current
legislative exercise presents a historic opportunity to resolve a decade-long legal
ambiguity. For years, the lack of a modern, flexible statutory framework has
created structural friction between traditional taxi operators holding premiumvalue licenses and electronic platform drivers operating under shifting enforcement
priorities.A regulatory approach focused purely on restriction or protectionism
misses the root cause of shifting passenger preferences. Rather than viewing Uber
and conventional taxis as a zero-sum conflict, a forward-looking legal framework
should embrace regulatory coexistence, a model successfully executed in
jurisdictions worldwide to maximize consumer welfare and administrative
oversight.3. The Passenger Mandate: Resolving the "Not User-Friendly" Taxi
ExperienceA primary catalyst for the widespread adoption of ride-hailing services
in Hong Kong is the persistent deficit in service quality and user accessibility
within the traditional taxi trade. For years, passengers have voiced consistent
dissatisfaction regarding several systemic issues:* **Refusal of Fares and CherryPicking:** Drivers frequently decline short-haul trips, journeys through crossharbor tunnels during peak hours, or destinations perceived as less lucrative.*

**Inflexible Payment Ecosystems:** Despite Hong Kong’s status as a leading
global financial center, a significant portion of the traditional taxi fleet continues to
rely exclusively on cash payments, creating immense friction for digital-first
residents and international tourists alike.* **Behavioral and Condition
Deficiencies:** Complaints regarding overcharging, poor vehicle hygiene,
aggressive driving behavior, and a general lack of accountability have continually
tarnished the industry’s reputation.In stark contrast, ride-hailing platforms leverage

Submission to the Subcommittee on Four Pieces of Subsidiary Legislation Relating
to Regulation of Ride-Hailing Services
I am a daily user of ride-hailing services in Hong Kong. I submit these views
because the proposed subsidiary legislation, while bringing long-overdue
regulation, is overly restrictive, favours the taxi trade, and will harm the many
passengers who currently receive significantly better service, cleaner vehicles, and
more professional conduct from ride-hailing platforms.
Hong Kong’s traditional taxi service suffers well-documented systemic problems.
Rudeness tops the list of complaints in official surveys. Many vehicles are old,
dirty, and poorly maintained. A large proportion of drivers are elderly — around
60% were aged 60 or above in recent data — raising legitimate concerns about
reaction times and driving standards. Taxis have also been involved in a
disproportionately high share of traffic accidents. These issues are not isolated;
they reflect structural weaknesses in an ageing fleet and workforce with limited
direct accountability.
In contrast, ride-hailing consistently delivers superior outcomes for passengers.
The app provides transparency, real-time tracking, cashless payment, and a twoway rating system that enforces better driver behaviour and vehicle standards.
Most users experience cleaner cars, politer drivers, and safer, more professional
driving. This quality advantage is the reason many people, including myself,
choose ride-hailing daily.
The four pieces of subsidiary legislation fail to reflect these realities. The proposed
cap of 10,000 vehicle permits is far too low. Passenger surveys already show
strong demand for greater supply, with the vast majority describing the 10,000
limit as insufficient. A tight cap will lead to longer waiting times, higher fares, and
reduced choice — outcomes that contradict the government’s aim of facilitating

public travel needs.
The owner-driver requirement, high platform licence fees, and rigid licensing
structure further constrain supply and flexibility. These measures go beyond
reasonable safety and quality standards. They function primarily as barriers that
protect incumbent taxi interests rather than maximising safe, high-quality point-topoint transport for the public.
A fair and equitable regime should apply rigorous but outcome-focused standards
— safety record, medical fitness, vehicle condition, and conduct — equally to all
operators. Competent existing taxi drivers should be able to obtain ride-hailing
permits through the combined test without unnecessary obstacles. Supply should
be guided primarily by passenger demand and safety performance, supported by
transparent, data-driven adjustment mechanisms rather than a hard low cap.
I therefore urge the Subcommittee to recommend a significantly higher initial
permit cap, or a clear and responsive mechanism to increase numbers based on
wait times and passenger demand; substantial easing of the owner-driver
restriction; and strong, consistent standards applied equally across both sectors.
The goal of regulation should be safer, cleaner, and more reliable transport for the
people of Hong Kong — not the preservation of an underperforming status quo.

主旨：反對批出 10,000 個網約車牌

本人就政府建議首階段批出 10,000 個網約車牌一事，表示強烈反對。此舉
對的士行業衝擊過大，數量亦明顯過多，欠缺審慎評估，將嚴重影響市場公
平及從業員生計。

現時全港的士約 18,000 輛，若短時間內新增 10,000 輛網約車，點對點載
客服務供應將激增逾五成，遠超實際需求增長，必然導致供過於求，司機收
入被迅速攤薄，市場陷入惡性競爭。

的士司機絕大部分為自負盈虧的個體經營者，無固定工資，全靠每日車程維
生，並需承擔車租、燃料、保險、維修等高額開支。一旦車程減少，收入立
刻下降，甚至無法應付基本生活。10,000 輛網約車同時進入市場，乘客將
被大幅分流，導致的士司機車程銳減、收入急跌，令大量司機經營困難，甚
至被迫離場。

更嚴重的是，大規模發牌將間接扶植大型網約車平台。平台憑資金優勢、演
算法派單及補貼推廣，可迅速吸納司機和乘客，擴大市場控制力。當市場份
額集中於少數平台，點對點交通服務將逐步形成寡頭壟斷。

一旦形成壟斷，平台在搶佔市場初期以優惠、補貼吸引用戶；待傳統行業被
削弱後，便可能提高抽成、調整收費機制、削減司機分成。屆時，司機由自

主經營變成高度依賴平台派單，議價能力極低；乘客亦可能面對收費上升、
服務不穩及選擇減少。換言之，市場主導權將集中於少數商業平台，而非真
正提升服務。

同時，網約車平台可透過補貼、優惠及動態定價吸引乘客，但的士司機無法
按市場即時調價，亦難以長期補貼。這種不對等競爭，使的士客源持續被蠶
食。若政府以大規模發牌進一步擴張平台車輛數目，無異於加速淘汰傳統的
士，變相協助平台擴大市場支配力。

此外，不少司機年齡偏高，轉職能力有限。若行業短期內被急速擠壓，大批
從業員將難以重新投入勞動市場，引發失業、貧窮及家庭經濟壓力等連鎖問
題。當局所謂「動態調整配額」機制，實際運作滯後，難以及時補救；當司
機因收入崩塌退出市場時，行業結構已難以逆轉。

基於以上原因，首階段批出 10,000 個網約車牌屬過急、過多，亦可能破壞
市場平衡，助長平台壟斷風險。本人強烈促請政府重新審視方案，將發牌數
目限制於不多於 5,000 個，作為試行計劃，並設立清晰檢討機制，於兩年
後根據實際營運數據、司機收入變化、市場集中程度及行業影響作全面評估
，再決定是否調整配額。

若政府忽視的士司機實際困境，貿然大規模發牌，將衝擊無數司機及家庭生
計，更可能令香港點對點交通服務市場逐步落入少數平台控制，後果深遠且
難以挽回。

I am writing to express my strong concerns regarding the proposed subsidiary
legislation for the regulation of ride-hailing services in Hong Kong, specifically the
implementation of a 10,000-vehicle permit cap and the associated allocation
mechanisms.
While I support the principle of bringing clarity to the legal status of ride-hailing
services, I believe the current proposal is impractical and fundamentally flawed in
its approach to managing urban mobility. My objections are based on the following
points:
1. A Rigid Cap Ignores Real-World Demand
Imposing an arbitrary ceiling of 10,000 vehicles fails to account for the dynamic
nature of passenger demand in a global city like Hong Kong. Demand for point-topoint transport is not constant; it spikes during inclement weather, late-night hours,
and large-scale public events. By artificially capping the supply, the government is
essentially guaranteeing longer wait times and inflated prices for the public, which
contradicts the goal of providing a "diverse and efficient" transport ecosystem.
2. Inequitable Regulatory Burden
The proposal appears to be a lopsided restriction that unfairly targets new mobility
providers. While the government is quick to impose strict quotas and operational
constraints on ride-hailing, there is a lack of equivalent, robust enforcement
regarding the service quality of the traditional taxi industry—a sector that
continues to face public criticism over fare refusals, service quality, and a lack of
modernization. It is inconsistent to stifle a more tech-enabled and service-oriented
sector with restrictive quotas while failing to apply the same level of rigorous
service-level controls to incumbent providers.

3. Hindering Innovation and Consumer Choice
Hong Kong prides itself on being a free-market economy, yet this proposal
introduces a "lottery-based" allocation system that ignores market efficiency. A
system that restricts supply rather than fostering healthy competition will
ultimately penalize the commuter. We should be moving toward a model that
encourages competition and service quality, not one that protects outdated models
through supply-side suppression.
I urge the Council to reconsider the 10,000-vehicle quota and move toward a more
flexible, demand-responsive licensing model. Regulations should focus on safety,
insurance, and service quality standards, rather than placing an artificial lid on the
number of vehicles available to the public. If the government is truly committed to
a "people-oriented" transport policy, it must prioritize the convenience and rights
of the commuting public over the protection of existing market structures.

Chinese or Cantonese. If they are unable to obtain permits, their employment
opportunities may become severely limited, as language barriers make it difficult
to transition into other local jobs.

newer, safer cars. However, uncertainty about whether they will receive permits
discourages them from making this financial commitment. A clearer and more
predictable system would encourage upgrades and improve overall service quality.
•A transparent and fair allocation system is essential to maintain trust, ensure
safety, and support both drivers and passengers in the long term.

while ensuring that ride-hailing services remain reliable, accessible, and
sustainable.
