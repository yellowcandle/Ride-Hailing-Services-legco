# Analysis of Submissions — 與規管網約車服務有關的4項附屬法例小組委員會 (sc103)

Snapshot: 2026-06-11 (uploads as of 2026-06-10; invitation closes 2026-06-15 17:00).
Coverage: all 16 public PDFs read in full — 7 organisation submissions + 9 bundled volumes containing 423 individual public submissions with content (papers CB(3)662/2026(03)–(427)). 27 further entries are 只限委員參閱 (members only) and could not be read.

---

## 1. Overall stance toward the proposed subsidiary legislation

### Individual public submissions (n = 423)

| Stance | Count | % |
|---|---|---|
| Support regulation AND the rules as proposed | 28 | ~7% |
| **Support regulating ride-hailing BUT object to specific provisions** (overwhelmingly the 10,000-vehicle quota) | **352** | **~83%** |
| Oppose the regulatory regime outright | 24 | ~6% |
| Unclear / no stance on the legislation | 19 | ~4% |

The single dominant message: **~323 of 423 (≈76%) say the 10,000 vehicle-permit cap is far too few** — either raise it (most common asks: 20,000–30,000; range 15,000–300,000) or abolish it and let the market decide (~60 submissions). This is broadly consistent with the Ming Pao report of "約六成挺1萬牌太少" (their count was more conservative).

| Point | ≈n | Direction |

### Submitter profile (individuals)
- Passengers / general public: ~85–90% (incl. a visible cohort of expat/ethnic-minority residents writing in English)

- Taxi-industry-aligned individuals: ~5

## 2. The parties, grouped

### A. Platform / pro-ride-hailing industry — POSITIVE on the framework, NEGATIVE on key details (want loosening)

**Uber 香港團隊** [CB(3)634/2026(01)]
- Welcomes the regulatory framework as "an important step".
- 10,000 cap covers under ⅓ of its >30,000 active drivers; >20,000 would lose income. Urges rapid dynamic review (at least quarterly) using P90 metrics with objective trigger thresholds, before full implementation in Aug 2027.
- Allocation: priority to experienced/active drivers, lottery only as tie-breaker; ≤20% of quota for vehicle-ownership-based priority (≈40% of drivers don't own the car they drive).
- Phased licensing: provisional allocation first, buy/register compliant car within ~3 months after.
- Insurance: amend/work around s.55T so platform-purchased block ("統括") insurance is possible; verification by platforms, not at permit stage.

**智慧交通聯盟 (Smart Mobility/Transport Alliance)** [CB(3)662/2026(01)]
- "Fully supports" the regime and the 總量管控、動態評估 principles.
- Calculates real need >15,200 active vehicles vs 10,000 permits; wants reviews every 2–3 months initially (relaxing to 6–12 months later), published publicly.
- Priority licensing for existing drivers; quantified minimum-activity rules to purge idle permits; ban permit resale/speculation; supports 人車綁定 with strict biometric-data safeguards; proposes a separate pilot quota for autonomous vehicles.

### B. Labour — POSITIVE overall (welcome legalisation), focused on driver protections

**香港運輸物流業工會聯會 (affiliated with 勞聯/Federation of HK & Kowloon Labour Unions, not FTU)** [CB(3)641/2026(01)]
- "Welcomes and supports" legalisation; accepts 10,000 as a prudent starting point but wants a licence-number review mechanism and a crackdown on permit speculation.
- Priority licensing by platform work record/ratings; protect peak-hour part-timers.
- Driver rights: platform commissions (27–30%) too high; statutory work-injury/retirement protection; a third "independent worker" legal status; platform-purchased third-party insurance; standardised, transparent platform fees; rest stations and dedicated stopping bays; tripartite (labour-platform-government) consultation body.

### C. Taxi industry — accept regulation exists, but NEGATIVE on the package as drafted (want tightening)

**的士車行車主協會 (Taxi Dealers & Owners Association)** [CB(3)634/2026(02)]
- Taxi ridership halved (1.3M → 660k/day); 400–500 idle "死牌". Releasing 10,000 permits would be "毀滅性打擊".
- Wants: phased (not one-off) issuance with downward dynamic adjustment; ride-hailing costs/rules aligned with taxis (vehicle age ≤3 years, fares not below taxi, visible markings, no taxi-only zones); drastically heavier penalties on white-plate cars, drivers, vehicles (confiscation) and platforms (punitive per-order fines, licence revocation, data localisation).

**香港的士業議會 (Hong Kong Taxi Council)** [CB(3)641/2026(02)]
- Principle: 「的士為主、網約車為輔」. Supports regulation but says current details don't relieve industry fears.
- Wants: small-step (小步快走) quota releases — disputes the government's demand estimates; cap each driver at ≤20 online hours/week to keep ride-hailing part-time; a multi-stakeholder review committee (not waiting-time-based triggers); uniform insurance/inspection standards (designated centres); a taxi-style demerit system + mandatory cameras ("三寶") for ride-hailing; ban using 禁區紙 for commercial point-to-point service.

**香港的士小巴商總會 (HK Taxi & Public Light Bus Association)** [CB(3)641/2026(03) revised]
- Identifies "four core defects": no statutory hour cap, permit fee too low, platform annual fee too low, no fare regulation.
- Wants: statutory 20-hour/week hard cap enforced by a government central server aggregating cross-platform online hours (detailed appendix); platform annual fee raised from $1.2M to ≥$5M + 1–2% of revenue; statutory minimum fare not below the taxi meter; argues $1,560 permit fee assumes part-time use and under-recovers public costs if drivers go full-time.

### D. Civil society / think-tank-style — POSITIVE on legalisation, NEGATIVE on quota mechanism

**香港文化教育聯合會 (HK Culture & Education Federation)** [CB(3)662/2026(02)]
- Affirms legalisation but argues hard quotas create artificial scarcity, surge pricing, and rent-seeking; lottery is the least efficient allocator.
- Wants quantity control replaced by quality control: no cap, high entry standards (vehicle age, training, clean record, full commercial insurance), strong post-licensing supervision (data-driven demerit/suspension system, platform joint liability, shared anonymised traffic data).

### E. Individual public (423) — see §1: overwhelmingly pro-ride-hailing passengers demanding a higher/no quota.

## 3. Positive / negative scorecard

Two axes matter: (a) attitude to regulating ride-hailing at all, and (b) attitude to the 4 pieces of subsidiary legislation as drafted.

| Party | Regulating at all | The rules as drafted | Direction of change sought |
|---|---|---|---|
| Uber | ✅ positive | ⚠️ negative on quota, lottery, ownership rule, s.55T insurance | Loosen / redesign |
| 智慧交通聯盟 | ✅ positive | ⚠️ negative on quota adequacy | Loosen (faster dynamic review) |
| 香港文化教育聯合會 | ✅ positive | ❌ negative on quota+lottery mechanism | Replace quota with quality bar |
| 港運聯 (union) | ✅ positive | 🟡 broadly accepts; wants additions | Add driver protections |
| 的士車行車主協會 | ✅ (wants stricter) | ❌ negative — too generous to ride-hailing | Tighten sharply |
| 香港的士業議會 | ✅ (wants stricter) | ❌ negative — details don't protect taxis | Tighten (20h cap, parity rules) |
| 的士小巴商總會 | ✅ (wants stricter) | ❌ negative — "four core defects" | Tighten (hour cap, fees, fare floor) |
| Individual public (n=423) | ✅ ~90% positive | ❌ ~83% object to provisions (mainly quota) | Loosen (raise/abolish quota) |

**Net picture:** Almost nobody disputes that ride-hailing should be legalised and regulated. The fight is over calibration, in opposite directions: the taxi trade says the regime is far too loose (quota too high, fees too low, no hour cap, no fare floor), while platforms, the alliance, civil-society groups and the overwhelming majority of ~423 public submitters say it is far too tight (quota far too low, lottery arbitrary, insurance/ownership/demerit rules unworkable). The unions sit in the middle, focused on driver welfare. The most contested single number is the 10,000-permit cap; the second-most contested design choice is lottery-based allocation versus priority for existing active drivers.
